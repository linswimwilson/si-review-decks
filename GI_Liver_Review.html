I
Hepatobiliary System
The liver, bile, gallbladder & pancreas
Hepatobiliary System
The liver does everything.
Largest internal organ. Highly vascular. CAN regenerate.
Unique dual blood supply: hepatic artery (O2) + portal vein (nutrients from GI).
Liver Functions

What the Liver Does — Part 1

  • Detoxifies: metabolizes drugs, toxins, alcohol
  • Synthesizes: albumin, clotting factors, glucose, proteins, cholesterol
  • Stores: glycogen, fats, nutrients — releases when needed
  • Makes bile → emulsifies fat for absorption
Think of the liver as a factory, warehouse, and recycling center all in one.
Liver Functions

What the Liver Does — Part 2

  • Converts ammonia (NH3) → urea for kidney excretion
  • Breaks down hormones (estrogen, testosterone)
  • Removes old RBCs — recycles iron & proteins
  • Maintains blood volume via albumin (oncotic pressure holds fluid in vessels)
When the Liver Fails

Map Each Failure to Its Symptom

  • Can't clear drugs → toxicity
  • Can't make albumin → edema, ascites
  • Can't make clotting factors → bleeding, bruising
  • Can't clear bilirubin → jaundice, pruritus
  • Can't convert ammonia → hepatic encephalopathy
  • Can't break down estrogen → males: gynecomastia, hair loss
Quick Check
A patient has low albumin levels. What symptom would you expect?
Jaundice
Edema / ascites
Hepatic encephalopathy
Gynecomastia
Albumin maintains oncotic pressure — it holds fluid inside blood vessels. Low albumin = fluid leaks out = edema & ascites.
Bile & Gallbladder

Bile: Made by Liver, Stored in Gallbladder

  • Bile released into duodenum when fat is present
  • Emulsifies fat → allows absorption of fat + fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K
  • No bile = steatorrhea (floating, greasy, foul stool) + ADEK deficiency
  • Vitamin K deficiency = no clotting (most dangerous)
No gallbladder? Bile still flows continuously — but patient can't handle large fatty meals.
Bile & Gallbladder

Gallbladder Clinical Pearls

  • Gallbladder sits under the liver — NOT normally palpable
  • Gallbladder pain is referred pain between the shoulders
  • Ducts from gallbladder + pancreas enter duodenum side by side
Gallstones → block bile duct → inflame pancreatic duct → enzymes auto-digest pancreas → pancreatitis
Pancreas

Exocrine Pancreas

  • Digestive enzymes — break down carbs, proteins, fats
  • Bicarbonate (HCO3−) — neutralizes acidic chyme from stomach
  • Gallstones can block the shared duct → pancreatic enzymes activate inside the pancreas → auto-digestion = pancreatitis
Nursing Pearl

Newborns & Vitamin K

  • Newborn gut is sterile — no bacteria to make Vitamin K
  • Without Vit K, minor injury = major bleed
  • Vit K injection at birth — standard of care
  • Takes ~3 months for gut bacteria to colonize and produce Vit K
Quick Check
A gallstone blocks the bile duct. Which vitamin deficiency is the MOST dangerous?
Vitamin A — vision problems
Vitamin D — bone weakness
Vitamin E — nerve damage
Vitamin K — bleeding/no clotting
No bile = no fat absorption = no ADEK. Vitamin K is the most immediately dangerous because it's needed for clotting factors. Without it, the patient can bleed out.
II
Hepatitis
Inflammation of the liver — viral & non-viral causes
Hepatitis
Hepatitis destroys liver cells.
Necrosis → scarring → cirrhosis → cancer.
The endpoint is always the same. The cause determines the timeline.
Hepatitis Types

Vowels: A & E

  • Oral-fecal route (contaminated food/water)
  • Self-limiting — no chronic form
  • Hep A: vaccine (2-dose)
  • Hep E: no vaccine, rare in US

Consonants: B, C, D

  • Blood & body fluids (HCW at greater risk)
  • Hep B: vaccine (3-dose), CAN become chronic
  • Hep C: NO vaccine, most likely → chronic → cirrhosis
  • Hep D: only co-infects with B
Memory trick: Vowels = the vowel in “fecal.” Consonants = blood.
Hepatitis

Clinical Course

  • Prodromal: fatigue, anorexia, nausea, RUQ discomfort (may be asymptomatic)
  • Icteric: jaundice, dark urine, clay-colored stools, pruritus, elevated AST/ALT
  • Recovery: weeks to months. A & E resolve. B & C may become chronic.
Elevated AST/ALT = liver cell damage. Elevated bilirubin = liver can't do its job.
Hepatitis

Non-Viral Hepatitis

  • Alcohol — most common non-viral cause
  • Tylenol (acetaminophen) — toxic to liver in high doses
  • Seizure meds, antibiotics — drug-induced
  • Autoimmune hepatitis
Non-viral hepatitis is NOT contagious — but the liver damage is the same.
Hepatitis Treatment & Prevention

Keep It Simple

  • Vaccines: Hep A (2-dose) and Hep B (3-dose)
  • Hygiene/sanitation for A & E
  • Universal precautions, safe sex, sterile needles for B, C, D
  • Supportive care — rest, hydration, nutrition
  • No Tylenol. No alcohol.
Quick Check
A traveler returns from Central America with jaundice, fatigue, and dark urine. No IV drug history. Most likely?
Hepatitis A — oral-fecal, travel exposure
Hepatitis B — blood/body fluid transmission
Hepatitis C — blood transmission, no vaccine
Hepatitis D — co-infection with B
Travel + contaminated food/water + no IV drug history = oral-fecal route = Hep A (vowel!). Self-limiting, vaccine-preventable.

Cirrhosis
Cirrhosis = three simultaneous failures
Bilirubin rises. Albumin drops. Ammonia accumulates.
Learn these three and you can explain every cirrhosis symptom.
Collapse 1

↑ Bilirubin: Can't Conjugate or Excrete

  • Jaundice — yellow skin and sclera
  • Pruritus — bile salts deposit in skin (intense itching)
  • Dark urine — excess bilirubin excreted by kidneys
  • Clay-colored stools — no bilirubin reaches the gut
Collapse 2

↓ Albumin + Portal Hypertension

  • Low albumin = low oncotic pressure → fluid leaks out
  • Fibrosis blocks portal vein → portal hypertension
  • Ascites — fluid fills abdomen (can push diaphragm → shallow breathing)
  • Varices — esophageal varices, hemorrhoids (blood reroutes around liver)
  • Peripheral edema
Esophageal varices can rupture — life-threatening GI bleed.
Collapse 3

↑ Ammonia: Can't Convert NH3 → Urea

  • Ammonia accumulates in blood → crosses blood-brain barrier
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: confusion → lethargy → unresponsive
  • Asterixis — "flapping tremor" of the hands (classic sign)
Test for asterixis: ask patient to extend arms & dorsiflex wrists. Watch for involuntary flapping.
Cirrhosis

Other Cirrhosis Failures

  • Hormone breakdown fails → males: gynecomastia, hair loss; females: masculine features
  • Clotting fails → bruising, bleeding, elevated INR
  • Dilated abdominal veins (caput medusae)
  • Low blood sugar — liver can't release stored glucose
  • Delayed drug clearance — medications build up
Cirrhosis Treatment

Nursing Priorities

  • Encephalopathy: lactulose (traps NH3 in gut), limit protein
  • Ascites: diuretics, sodium restriction, paracentesis (drain fluid)
  • Varices: monitor for bleeding (can be fatal)
  • No Tylenol. No alcohol. Ever.
  • Liver transplant is the only cure (liver CAN regenerate — living donor possible)
Quick Check
A cirrhosis patient has confusion and asterixis. Which liver failure is responsible?
↑ Bilirubin — can't conjugate/excrete
↓ Albumin — low oncotic pressure
↑ Ammonia — can't convert NH₃ to urea
↓ Clotting factors — bleeding risk
Confusion + asterixis (flapping tremor) = hepatic encephalopathy. Ammonia crosses the blood-brain barrier when the liver can't convert it to urea. Treat with lactulose.
Clinical Case

Robert, 58 y/o Male

History of chronic Hep C. Presents with distended abdomen, yellow sclera, confusion, and easy bruising.

Labs: low albumin, high bilirubin, high ammonia, elevated INR.

Map each finding to the failed liver job.
  • Distended abdomen (ascites) → low albumin + portal HTN (fluid leaks out)
  • Yellow sclera (jaundice) → high bilirubin (can't conjugate/excrete)
  • Confusion → high ammonia (crosses BBB → encephalopathy)
  • Easy bruising + elevated INR → can't make clotting factors
  • Low albumin → liver can't synthesize proteins
Every symptom maps to a specific liver job that failed. This is the framework — learn the jobs, and you can explain any cirrhosis presentation.
Summary
Three Big Takeaways
1. The liver does everything. When it fails, map each symptom to the specific job.
2. Hepatitis: vowels (A, E) = oral-fecal. Consonants (B, C, D) = blood & body fluids.
3. Cirrhosis = three collapses: ↑ bilirubin, ↓ albumin/portal HTN, ↑ ammonia.
