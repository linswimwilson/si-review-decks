Section A

Hepatobiliary System

The liver, bile, gallbladder & pancreas
Core Concept

The Liver Does Everything.

Largest internal organ. Highly vascular. CAN regenerate. Dual blood supply: hepatic artery (O₂) + portal vein (nutrients from GI).
Liver Functions

What the Liver Does — Part 1

☢
Detoxifies
  • Metabolizes drugs, toxins
  • Processes alcohol
🛠
Synthesizes
  • Albumin, clotting factors
  • Glucose, proteins, cholesterol
📦
Stores
  • Glycogen, fats, nutrients
  • Releases when needed
💧
Makes Bile
  • Emulsifies fat for absorption
Think of the liver as a factory, warehouse, and recycling center all in one.
Liver Functions

What the Liver Does — Part 2

NH₃

  • Urea excreted by kidneys
♀

  • Estrogen, testosterone
♻
Removes Old RBCs
  • Recycles iron & proteins
💥
Maintains Blood Volume
  • Albumin → oncotic pressure
  • Holds fluid inside vessels

Quick Check
A patient has low albumin levels. What symptom would you expect?
A. Jaundice
Jaundice is from high bilirubin, not low albumin. Different liver function.
B. Edema / ascites
Correct. Albumin maintains oncotic pressure — it holds fluid inside blood vessels. Low albumin = fluid leaks out = edema and ascites.
C. Hepatic encephalopathy
That’s from ammonia buildup (the liver can’t convert NH₃ to urea), not albumin.
D. Gynecomastia
That’s from failure to break down estrogen, not from low albumin.
Bile & Gallbladder

Bile: Made by Liver, Stored in Gallbladder

💧
Where Bile Goes

  • Emulsifies fat → absorption
ADEK
Fat-Soluble Vitamins
  • A, D, E, K absorbed with fat
  • No bile → no ADEK
💩
No Bile
  • Steatorrhea (floating, greasy, foul stool)

No gallbladder? Bile still flows continuously — but patient can’t handle large fatty meals.

Quick Check
A gallstone blocks the bile duct. Which vitamin deficiency is the MOST dangerous?
A. Vitamin A — vision problems
Vit A deficiency is real here (no fat absorption = no ADEK) but not the most immediately dangerous.
B. Vitamin D — bone weakness
Important, but develops slowly. Not the acute threat.
C. Vitamin E — nerve damage
Also slow to develop. Not the most urgent.
D. Vitamin K — bleeding / no clotting
Correct. No bile = no fat absorption = no ADEK. Vit K is needed for clotting factors. Without it, the patient can bleed out — most immediate danger.
Section B

Hepatitis

Inflammation of the liver — viral and non-viral causes
Core Concept

Hepatitis Destroys Liver Cells.

Necrosis → scarring → cirrhosis → cancer. The endpoint is always the same. The cause determines the timeline.

Hepatitis Types

Vowels: A & E

  • Oral-fecal route (contaminated food/water)
  • Self-limiting — no chronic form
  • Hep A: 2-dose vaccine available
  • Hep E: no vaccine, rare in US

Consonants: B, C, D

  • Blood & body fluids (HCW at greater risk)
  • Hep B: 3-dose vaccine; CAN become chronic
  • Hep C: NO vaccine; most likely to become chronic → cirrhosis
  • Hep D: only co-infects with B
Memory trick: The vowel is in the bowel — A and E travel through the bowel (oral-fecal). Consonants = blood and body fluids.
Hepatitis

Clinical Course

1
Prodromal Phase
  • Fatigue, anorexia, nausea
  • RUQ discomfort
  • May be asymptomatic
2
Icteric Phase
  • Jaundice, dark urine
  • Clay-colored stools, pruritus
  • Elevated AST/ALT
3
Recovery
  • Weeks to months
  • A & E resolve fully
  • B & C may become chronic
Elevated AST/ALT = liver cell damage. Elevated bilirubin = liver can’t do its job.
Hepatitis

Non-Viral Hepatitis

🍺
Alcohol (#1)
  • Most common non-viral cause
💊
Tylenol (Acetaminophen)
  • Toxic to liver in high doses
Rx
Drug-Induced
  • Seizure meds
  • Some antibiotics
🦠
Autoimmune
  • Body attacks liver tissue
Non-viral hepatitis is NOT contagious — but the liver damage is the same.
Hepatitis

Treatment & Prevention

💉
Vaccines
  • Hep A — 2-dose series
  • Hep B — 3-dose series
  • No vaccine for C, D, E
🧹
Prevention — A/E
  • Hygiene & sanitation
  • Clean food/water
🚪
Prevention — B/C/D
  • Universal precautions
  • Safe sex, sterile needles
❌
Absolute No’s
  • No Tylenol
  • No alcohol
Supportive care — rest, hydration, nutrition. For chronic B/C, antivirals can suppress viral load.
Quick Check
A traveler returns from Central America with jaundice, fatigue, and dark urine. No IV drug history. Most likely?
A. Hepatitis A
Correct! Travel + contaminated food/water + no IV drug history = oral-fecal route = Hep A (the vowel!). Self-limiting, vaccine-preventable.
B. Hepatitis B
Hep B is blood/body fluid transmission. The travel + no IV drugs history makes this less likely.
C. Hepatitis C
Hep C is blood transmission — no vaccine. Not consistent with the travel-exposure pattern.
D. Hepatitis D
Hep D only co-infects with B. Less likely from travel exposure alone.

Framework

Cirrhosis = Three Simultaneous Failures

Bilirubin rises. Albumin drops. Ammonia accumulates. Learn these three and you can explain every cirrhosis symptom.
Collapse 1

↑ Bilirubin — Can’t Conjugate or Excrete

🐌
Jaundice
  • Yellow skin and sclera
🤸
Pruritus
  • Bile salts deposit in skin
  • Intense itching
💧
Dark Urine
  • Excess bilirubin excreted by kidneys
🩕
Clay-Colored Stools
  • No bilirubin reaching the gut
Collapse 2

↓ Albumin + Portal HTN → Ascites & 3rd Spacing

↓
Low Albumin (Oncotic)
  • Can’t hold fluid IN vessels
  • Fluid leaks out into tissues
↑
Portal HTN (Hydrostatic)
  • Fibrosis blocks portal vein
  • Pressure pushes fluid out
🪁
Ascites
  • Fluid pools in peritoneum
  • Pushes diaphragm up → dyspnea
🪩
3rd Spacing
  • Fluid trapped in tissues, NOT circulation
  • Looks wet, intravascularly DRY
…
Also: Varices
  • Blood reroutes around the blocked liver → esophageal varices, hemorrhoids
The paradox: patient is fluid-overloaded peripherally but hypovolemic centrally. BP can be low even with massive ascites. Monitor volume status by BP, HR, urine output — not by how wet the patient looks.
Collapse 3

↑ Ammonia — Can’t Convert NH3 → Urea

🧠
NH₃ Crosses Blood-Brain Barrier
  • Ammonia accumulates in blood → crosses BBB
  • Neurotoxic
💦
Hepatic Encephalopathy
  • Confusion → lethargy → unresponsive
🤚
Asterixis
  • “Flapping tremor” of the hands
  • Classic sign of encephalopathy
Test for asterixis: ask patient to extend arms & dorsiflex wrists. Watch for involuntary flapping.
Cirrhosis

Other Cirrhosis Failures

♂
Hormone Breakdown Fails
  • Males: gynecomastia, hair loss
  • Females: masculine features
💥
Clotting Fails
  • Bruising, bleeding
  • Elevated INR
🧐
Caput Medusae
  • Dilated abdominal veins from portal HTN backup
↓
Low Blood Sugar
  • Liver can’t release stored glucose
Rx
Delayed Drug Clearance
  • Medications build up in the body
  • Increased risk of toxicity
Cirrhosis Treatment

Nursing Priorities

💦
Encephalopathy
  • Lactulose — traps NH₃ in gut
  • Limit dietary protein
🪁
Ascites
  • Diuretics, sodium restriction
  • Paracentesis to drain fluid
❌
Absolute No’s
  • No Tylenol. Ever.
  • No alcohol. Ever.
🔧
Liver Transplant
  • Only cure
  • Liver CAN regenerate — living donor possible
Monitor for variceal bleeding — can be fatal. Be ready with banding, sclerotherapy, or balloon tamponade.
Quick Check
A cirrhosis patient has confusion and asterixis. Which liver failure is responsible?
A. ↑ Bilirubin — can’t conjugate/excrete
Bilirubin causes jaundice, pruritus, dark urine, clay stools — not the flapping tremor or confusion.
B. ↓ Albumin — low oncotic pressure
Low albumin causes edema and ascites, not neurological symptoms.
C. ↑ Ammonia — can’t convert NH₃ to urea
Correct. Confusion + asterixis (flapping tremor) = hepatic encephalopathy. Ammonia crosses the blood-brain barrier when the liver can’t convert it to urea. Treat with lactulose.
D. ↓ Clotting factors — bleeding risk
That causes bruising/bleeding, not confusion or asterixis.
Clinical Scenario
Robert, 58M, history of chronic Hep C. Presents with distended abdomen, yellow sclera, confusion, and easy bruising. Labs: low albumin, high bilirubin, high ammonia, elevated INR.
Which of his findings maps to the ammonia failure specifically?
A. Distended abdomen
That’s ascites — from low albumin (oncotic) + portal HTN (hydrostatic). Not ammonia.
B. Yellow sclera
That’s jaundice — from high bilirubin (can’t conjugate/excrete). Not ammonia.
C. Confusion
Correct! Confusion (and asterixis if present) = hepatic encephalopathy from ammonia crossing the BBB. Treatment: lactulose traps NH₃ in the gut.
D. Easy bruising + elevated INR
That’s the clotting factor failure — liver can’t synthesize them. Not ammonia.
Every symptom maps to a specific liver job that failed. Distended abdomen → albumin + portal HTN. Yellow sclera → bilirubin. Confusion → ammonia. Bruising + INR → clotting factors.
Big Picture

Liver — The Big Picture

• The liver does everything. When it fails, map each symptom to the specific job that failed.
• Hepatitis: vowel is in the bowel — A and E are oral-fecal. Consonants (B, C, D) = blood & body fluids.
• Cirrhosis = three collapses: ↑ bilirubin, ↓ albumin + portal HTN (ascites/3rd spacing), ↑ ammonia (encephalopathy).
• Ascites paradox: patient looks wet but is intravascularly dry. Monitor volume by BP, HR, urine output.
• Cirrhosis is irreversible. Transplant is the only cure. No Tylenol, no alcohol — ever.
