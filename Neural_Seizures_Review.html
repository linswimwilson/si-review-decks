Section A

What’s a Seizure?

Normal electrical rhythm disrupted — and what disrupts it
Core Concept

Seizure = Abnormal Electrical Activity

Originates in the motor cortex. Measured with EEG (20 channels). Epilepsy = pattern of abnormal neurons — often no known cause and no cure.
Seizures

What Disrupts the Rhythm

🪁
Trauma
  • Direct brain injury
  • Post-TBI seizures
💥
Ischemia / Hypoxia
  • Neurons need constant O₂
  • Stroke, hypoxia → disrupted firing
🍬
Glucose Extremes
  • Hypoglycemia — brain starves
  • Hyperglycemia — also disrupts firing
  • Spiral to Ch.10 Diabetes
Na⁺
Electrolyte Imbalance
  • Especially Na⁺ (hyponatremia)
  • Na⁺ shifts → brain cell swelling → seizure
  • Spiral to Ch.6 Sodium
Common thread: anything that messes with the Na⁺/K⁺ balance or the brain’s fuel supply can trigger abnormal firing. Find and fix the underlying cause.
Quick Check
A patient with chronic SIADH has serum Na⁺ of 118 mEq/L (normal 135–145) and develops a seizure. What is the mechanism?
A. Low Na⁺ directly damages motor cortex cells
Not direct damage. Na⁺ affects water movement across the blood-brain barrier.
B. Hyponatremia pulls water into brain cells → cellular swelling → abnormal firing
Correct. Low serum Na⁺ makes blood hypotonic relative to cells. Water shifts INTO brain cells → cerebral edema → disrupts normal electrical rhythm → seizure. Spiral to Ch.6 sodium balance.
C. Low Na⁺ prevents insulin release → hyperglycemic seizure
No. SIADH isn’t a diabetes mechanism.
D. Hyponatremia causes hypoxia → seizure
No. Hyponatremia doesn’t cause hypoxia. The mechanism is cellular swelling.
Section B

Classification

Focal vs generalized — how much of the brain is firing
Core Concept

Focal = One Part. Generalized = Whole Brain.

Focal seizures start in 1 part of the brain and often produce symptoms in 1 part of the body. Generalized seizures involve both hemispheres and cause loss of consciousness.
Focal Types

Focal vs. Generalized

Focal (Partial)

  • Brain: 1 part
  • Body: often 1 part
  • Awareness: usually preserved (partial aware); lost in complex focal
  • Examples: partial aware, complex focal, absence

Generalized

  • Brain: both hemispheres
  • Body: whole-body motion
  • Awareness: LOST
  • Examples: tonic, clonic, atonic, tonic-clonic
  • Can cause brain damage with prolonged episodes
Quick Check
A teacher notices a 9-year-old student frequently stares blankly for 5–10 seconds, then resumes activity as if nothing happened. Which seizure type?
A. Tonic-clonic
No. Tonic-clonic involves stiffening + jerking with loss of consciousness — visually obvious.
B. Absence seizure
Correct. Classic absence seizure: brief staring spell, can look like ADHD or daydreaming. Often missed. Common in kids.
C. Atonic
No. Atonic seizures cause sudden loss of muscle tone (drop attacks), not quiet staring.
D. Partial aware
Not quite. Partial aware usually has specific motor or sensory symptoms in 1 body part. Pure staring = absence.

Phases

The Three Phases

Aurapremonition — warning
→
Ictalactive seizure
→
Postictalconfused, tired, exhausted
🔎
Aura — Warning
  • A “premonition” something is coming
  • May be a smell, sound, feeling, or déjà-vu
  • Seizure-alert dogs can detect
⚡
Ictal — Active Seizure
  • The active seizure activity itself
  • Stiffening, jerking, staring, or fall — depends on type
😴
Postictal — After
  • Confused, tired, exhausted
  • Let them rest until they wake up on their own
Emergency

Status Epilepticus → Acidosis

Seizure lasting longer than 20 minutes. Uses tons of energy → metabolic acidosis → brain damage. This is a medical emergency — stop the seizure with IV meds.
Section D

Nursing Safety

Their safety AND our safety

Seizure Safety — DO vs DO NOT

DO

  • Side-lying (recovery position) — prevents aspiration
  • Open airway with head tilt back AFTER seizure activity stops
  • Remove hazards from the immediate area
  • Let them rest in the postictal phase — wake up on their own
  • Time the seizure — length matters (>20 min = status epilepticus)

DO NOT

  • NEVER put fingers or objects in the mouth — injury risk to YOU and the patient
  • NEVER restrain — let the seizure run its course; restraint can cause injury
  • Do not move the patient unless they’re in danger
Risks during a seizure: fall (especially atonic); aspiration (if eating or lying flat).
Patient Teaching

Patient & Family Teaching

❌
Do NOT Restrain
  • Family should know: do not hold them down
  • Clear the area; let it run its course
🔖
Medi-Alert Bracelet
  • Identifies the condition to first responders
🔍
Find the Cause
  • Workup: CT, MRI, bloodwork
  • Rule out treatable triggers
💊
Anti-Seizure Medication Adherence
  • Never skip doses
  • Sudden stop can trigger status epilepticus
Quick Check
A patient is having a generalized tonic-clonic seizure. Which nursing actions are appropriate? (Pick the best action below)
A. Position the patient on their side and remove hazards
Correct. Side-lying prevents aspiration. Removing hazards protects from injury. Open airway with head tilt AFTER seizure stops. Let them rest postictally. NEVER restrain or put objects in the mouth.
B. Insert a padded tongue blade between the teeth
NO. Never put anything in the mouth during a seizure. You can injure the patient’s teeth/jaw or get bitten yourself.
C. Restrain the patient to prevent injury
NO. Restraining during a seizure can cause broken bones or muscle injury. Let the seizure run its course in a safe space.
D. Administer CPR immediately
No. The patient is seizing, not in cardiac arrest. CPR would be wrong. Focus on safety positioning until the seizure stops.
Section E

Clinical Application

Put it all together
Clinical Scenario
You’re at the nurses’ station when a family member shouts for help. You enter the room to find a 44M patient on the floor next to the bed, rigid, then jerking rhythmically. His eyes are rolled back. The seizure started ~30 seconds ago. You know he has a history of epilepsy.
Which is your FIRST action?
A. Insert a bite block to prevent tongue injury
NEVER. Bite blocks / anything in the mouth during a seizure → risk of broken teeth, aspiration, and injury to you. This is old teaching — do not do it.
B. Turn him to side-lying and clear hazards from the area
Correct. First action: protect the airway with side-lying (prevents aspiration) and remove hazards so he doesn’t injure himself on furniture. Time the seizure. Don’t restrain. Open airway with head tilt AFTER it ends.
C. Hold his arms and legs still to prevent injury
NEVER restrain. You can cause fractures or muscle injury. Let the seizure run its course in a safe space.
D. Begin chest compressions
He’s seizing, not in cardiac arrest. CPR would be wrong. Focus on seizure safety.
If this seizure lasts longer than 20 minutes, it becomes status epilepticus — medical emergency, IV meds to stop it, watch for metabolic acidosis and brain damage.
Big Picture

Seizures — The Big Picture

• Seizure = abnormal electrical activity, usually starting in motor cortex. EEG confirms.
• Causes: trauma, ischemia, hypoxia, glucose extremes, electrolyte imbalance (especially Na⁺).
• Focal = 1 part (partial aware, complex focal, absence). Generalized = whole brain (tonic, clonic, atonic, tonic-clonic).
• Three phases: Aura → Ictal → Postictal. Let them rest until they wake up on their own.
• Status epilepticus (> 20 min) = emergency → acidosis → brain damage.
• Safety: side-lying, clear hazards, time it, let it run. NEVER restrain. NEVER put anything in the mouth.
• Their safety AND our safety.
