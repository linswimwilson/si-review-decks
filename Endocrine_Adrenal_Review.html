Section A

Adrenal Anatomy

Two glands, two layers, very different hormones
Adrenal Glands

Two Layers, Very Different Jobs

⚡
Medulla (inner)Epinephrine & norepinephrine — fight-or-flight
⚖
Cortex (outer)Steroids: mineralocorticoids, glucocorticoids, gonadocorticoids
Sit on top of each kidney. For Cushing’s and Addison’s, the cortex is what matters — it makes cortisol and aldosterone.
Cortex Hormones

The Two Cortex Hormones You Must Know

☀
Cortisol (glucocorticoid)
  • Raises blood glucose
  • Suppresses inflammation
  • Controls sleep/wake (↑ AM, ↓ PM)
  • Powers stress response
💧
Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid)
  • Retains Na⁺ & water
  • ↑ Volume & BP
  • Excretes K⁺ in urine
Link them: Cortisol = sugar, inflammation, stress. Aldosterone = salt, water, potassium.
Clinical Pearl

K⁺ = Heart Concern. Na⁺ = Brain Concern.

Aldosterone retains Na⁺ while excreting K⁺ — it’s a swap. Lose aldosterone and both go the wrong way.

The HPA Axis

Hypothalamus(brain) — senses cortisol, releases CRH
→
Anterior Pituitaryreleases ACTH
→
Adrenal Cortex(on top of kidneys) — releases …
→
CORTISOLthe hormone — circulates in blood, acts on target tissues
↻ Negative Feedback (sensing)
Cortisol is the end product. It circulates in the blood and acts on target tissues all over the body — muscle, liver, immune cells, etc. The hypothalamus senses the circulating level: when it’s high enough, CRH shuts off and the whole axis quiets. The brain isn’t the target — it’s the thermostat. Exogenous steroids trip this same sensor, which is why the adrenals go to sleep.
Quick Check
Aldosterone causes the kidneys to retain sodium and water. What happens to potassium?
A. Potassium is also retained
No. If aldosterone retained both, we’d have no way to excrete K⁺. Na⁺ and K⁺ move in opposite directions.
B. K⁺ is excreted in urine → blood K⁺ falls
Correct! Aldosterone retains Na⁺ while excreting K⁺ — it’s a swap. The Na/K relationship is key.
C. Potassium is unaffected by aldosterone
No. Aldosterone is the primary regulator of K⁺ excretion at the kidney.
D. Potassium moves into bone storage
No. Bone stores calcium, not potassium. K⁺ is primarily intracellular and shifts with acid-base, not storage.
Section B

Cushing’s Syndrome

Too much cortisol — the body in overdrive
Core Concept

Cushing’s = EXCESS Cortisol

Too much of everything cortisol does. Too much sugar. Too much Na⁺/water. Too much immune suppression. Bones break down.
Cushing’s

Quick Check
A patient on long-term prednisone for rheumatoid arthritis develops a round face, truncal weight gain, easy bruising, and blood glucose 210 mg/dL (normal: 70–130). What is this?
A. Addison’s disease from steroid use
Opposite. Addison’s is cortisol DEFICIENCY — weight loss, hypoglycemia, hypotension. Chronic steroid use does the reverse.
B. Normal side effects of aging
No. Moon face, purple striae, and steroid-induced hyperglycemia are not normal aging — they’re classic Cushing’s.
C. Iatrogenic Cushing’s syndrome
Correct! Moon face + truncal obesity + easy bruising + hyperglycemia in a patient on chronic steroids = textbook iatrogenic Cushing’s.
D. Hypothyroidism
No. Hypothyroidism causes weight gain but not moon face, striae, or steroid-induced hyperglycemia.
Cushing’s

Treatment — Match the Cause

  • If iatrogenic: GRADUAL tapering — let the adrenals wake back up
  • If tumor: surgery, radiation, or chemotherapy
  • Manage complications: hyperglycemia, infections, edema, osteoporosis, bleeding
  • Fall prevention (osteoporosis + muscle weakness = high fracture risk)
NEVER abruptly stop steroids. The adrenals are “asleep” from HPA suppression. Sudden withdrawal → adrenal crisis → potentially fatal.
Emergency

Never Abruptly Stop Steroids

After long-term steroid use, the HPA axis is suppressed and the adrenals have atrophied. Stopping cold = no cortisol = adrenal crisis = cardiovascular collapse.
Section C

Addison’s Disease

Too little cortisol — the body running on empty
Core Concept

Addison’s = DEFICIENT Cortisol & Aldosterone

Not enough of anything. Not enough sugar, not enough Na⁺/water retention, no stress response. The body can’t compensate.
Addison’s

Emergency

Adrenal Crisis = Medical Emergency

Na⁺ drops, K⁺ spikes, BP crashes, kidneys shut down. Addison’s patients cannot mount a normal stress response.
Adrenal Crisis

Recognize & Treat Fast

Triggers

What pushes a patient into crisis
  • Stress, illness, surgery
  • Abruptly stopping steroids
  • Missed dose in Addison’s patient
  • Trauma, infection, dehydration
All Addison’s patients need STRESS DOSING

Clinical Picture

The body collapsing
  • Profound fatigue, weakness
  • Severe hypotension, vascular collapse
  • Severe dehydration
  • Na⁺ ↓, K⁺ ↑, BP ↓, kidneys shut down
Tx: IV fluids + IV hydrocortisone + cardiac monitor

Quick Check
A patient with Addison’s disease would be expected to have which electrolyte pattern?
A. High Na⁺, low K⁺
That’s the Cushing’s pattern — the opposite. Excess aldosterone-like activity retains Na⁺ and dumps K⁺.
B. Low Na⁺, high K⁺
Correct! No aldosterone = kidneys can’t retain Na⁺ (drops) and can’t excrete K⁺ (rises).
C. High Ca⁺, low phosphate
No. That’s hyperparathyroidism. Addison’s primarily disrupts Na/K.
D. Normal electrolytes, elevated glucose
No. Addison’s causes HYPOglycemia (no cortisol to raise glucose) and abnormal Na/K.

Cushing’s vs. Addison’s

Cushing’s (EXCESS)

  • Cortisol: High
  • Glucose: High (steroid DM)
  • Na⁺: High (retained)
  • K⁺: Low (excreted)
  • BP: Hypertension
  • Weight: Truncal obesity
  • Skin: Thin, striae, bruises
  • Look: Moon face, buffalo hump
  • Tx: Taper steroids, surgery

Addison’s (DEFICIENT)

  • Cortisol: Low
  • Glucose: Low
  • Na⁺: Low (lost)
  • K⁺: High (retained)
  • BP: Hypotension
  • Weight: Weight loss
  • Skin: Bronze hyperpigmentation
  • Look: Thin, fatigued
  • Tx: Lifelong replacement
Section D

Clinical Application

Put it all together
Clinical Scenario
Rosa, 45F, has been on prednisone 40mg daily for 3 years for lupus. She presents with a round face, central obesity, purple stretch marks on her abdomen, blood glucose 245 mg/dL (random >200 = diabetes; normal: 70–130), and BP 162/98 (HTN stage 2). She asks if she can just stop the prednisone because of the side effects.
What is the diagnosis, and what should you tell her?
A. Addison’s — stop the prednisone immediately
Wrong in both ways. This is Cushing’s (excess, not deficient), and stopping steroids abruptly could kill her.
B. Iatrogenic Cushing’s — GRADUAL taper only
Correct! Textbook iatrogenic Cushing’s: moon face, truncal obesity, purple striae, hyperglycemia, HTN. After 3 years, her adrenals are suppressed. Must taper gradually under medical supervision.
C. New-onset Type 2 diabetes only
The hyperglycemia is real but it’s steroid-induced. The moon face, striae, and truncal obesity point to Cushing’s.
D. Pituitary tumor — urgent surgery
Pituitary tumors can cause Cushing’s disease, but Rosa’s cause is obvious: 3 years of exogenous prednisone. No tumor needed.
Clinical Scenario
James, 34M, presents to the ED with profound fatigue, BP 78/50 (hypotensive), blood glucose 52 mg/dL (hypoglycemia <70), and dark bronze skin. Labs: Na⁺ 128 (normal 135–145), K⁺ 6.1 (normal 3.5–5.0). He reports salt cravings and has lost 20 lbs unintentionally.
What is the diagnosis, and what is the most immediately dangerous value?
A. Cushing’s — glucose 52 is the priority
Wrong diagnosis. Cushing’s causes HYPERglycemia and HYPERtension, not these values. James has every Addison’s hallmark.
B. Addison’s — Na⁺ 128 is the priority
Right diagnosis, wrong priority. Hyponatremia matters (brain concern) but K⁺ 6.1 is more immediately lethal.
C. Addison’s — K⁺ 6.1 is the priority (cardiac)
Correct! Every Addison’s hallmark is here. K⁺ 6.1 is the most immediately dangerous — hyperkalemia causes peaked T waves → wide QRS → VFib. Tx: IV fluids + IV hydrocortisone + cardiac monitor + correct electrolytes.
D. Adrenal crisis, no treatment needed
He IS in/near adrenal crisis — but it’s a medical emergency, not a watch-and-wait. Immediate fluids + hydrocortisone required.
Big Picture

Adrenal Disorders — The Big Picture

• Cortisol = sugar, inflammation, stress. Aldosterone = salt, water, potassium.
• Cushing’s = EXCESS: high glucose, high Na⁺, low K⁺, HTN, moon face, buffalo hump.
• Addison’s = DEFICIENT: low glucose, low Na⁺, high K⁺, hypotension, bronze skin, weight loss.
• NEVER abruptly stop steroids — gradual taper only.
• K⁺ = heart concern. Na⁺ = brain concern.
• Adrenal crisis is fatal without rapid fluids + hydrocortisone.
