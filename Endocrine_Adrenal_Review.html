Adrenal Anatomy

Two glands, two layers, very different hormones

Adrenal Glands — Structure

  • Located on top of each kidney — small but critical
  • Two distinct layers with completely different functions
  • Medulla (inner): produces epinephrine & norepinephrine (catecholamines) — fight-or-flight response
  • Cortex (outer): produces steroids — mineralocorticoids, glucocorticoids, gonadocorticoids
  • Think: the cortex is the layer that matters most for Cushing’s and Addison’s

The Two Cortex Hormones You Must Know

  • Cortisol (glucocorticoid): manages carb/fat/protein metabolism, suppresses inflammation, raises blood glucose, controls sleep/wake cycle (highest AM, lowest PM), boosts energy for stress response
  • Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid): causes kidneys to retain Na⁺ and water, increases intravascular volume and BP, excretes K⁺ in urine → blood K⁺ falls
Link them: Cortisol = sugar, inflammation, stress. Aldosterone = salt, water, potassium.

Quick Check

Aldosterone & Potassium

Aldosterone causes the kidneys to retain sodium and water. What happens to potassium?
A. Potassium is also retained
B. Potassium is excreted in urine, so blood potassium falls
C. Potassium is unaffected by aldosterone
D. Potassium moves into bone storage
B. Potassium is excreted in urine, so blood potassium falls. Aldosterone causes the kidneys to retain Na⁺ while excreting K⁺ — it’s a swap. The Na/K relationship is key. Bone storage is calcium, not potassium.

Cushing’s Syndrome

Too much cortisol — the body in overdrive

Cushing’s = EXCESS Cortisol

Think: too much of everything cortisol does.

Too much sugar (hyperglycemia). Too much sodium/water (edema, hypertension).
Too much immune suppression (infections, delayed healing).
Bones break down (osteoporosis).

Cushing’s — Causes

  • #1 cause: Iatrogenic — taking exogenous glucocorticoid medications (prednisone, dexamethasone) for chronic inflammatory conditions (asthma, arthritis, IBD)
  • Adrenal tumors secreting excess cortisol
  • Pituitary tumors secreting excess ACTH (Cushing’s disease specifically)
  • Paraneoplastic syndrome — ectopic ACTH secretion from cancer (e.g., small cell lung cancer)

Cushing’s — The Classic Look Think: Santa Claus

  • Truncal obesity with thin extremities
  • Moon face — round, full, red
  • Buffalo hump — fat pad on upper back
  • Purple striae (stretch marks) on abdomen
  • Thin, fragile skin that bruises easily
  • Muscle weakness and wasting
Instructor mnemonic: Does Santa have Cushing’s? Round face, big belly, red cheeks — yes.

Cushing’s — Metabolic Effects High Yield

  • Hyperglycemia → steroid-induced diabetes (cortisol raises blood sugar)
  • Hypertension + edema (cortisol has aldosterone-like effects → Na⁺/H₂O retention)
  • Hypokalemia (K⁺ excreted)
  • Osteoporosis (cortisol prevents bones from absorbing calcium → fracture risk)
  • Immunosuppression → infections, wounds that won’t heal
  • Mood changes, irritability, psychosis. Females: hirsutism. Males: hair loss.
Quick Check

Cushing’s Recognition

A patient on long-term prednisone for rheumatoid arthritis develops a round face, weight gain around the trunk, easy bruising, and a blood glucose of 210. What is this?
A. Addison's disease from steroid use
B. Normal side effects of aging
C. Iatrogenic Cushing's syndrome from exogenous glucocorticoids
D. Hypothyroidism
C. Iatrogenic Cushing’s syndrome. Moon face + truncal obesity + easy bruising + hyperglycemia in a patient on chronic steroids = textbook iatrogenic Cushing’s. Addison’s is cortisol DEFICIENCY (the opposite). Hypothyroidism causes weight gain but not this classic constellation.

Cushing’s — Treatment

  • If iatrogenic: GRADUAL tapering of glucocorticoids — let adrenals wake back up
  • If tumor: surgery, radiation, chemotherapy
  • Manage complications: hyperglycemia, infections, edema, osteoporosis, bleeding
  • Fall prevention (osteoporosis + muscle weakness = high fracture risk)
NEVER abruptly stop steroids! The adrenal glands are “asleep” from suppression. Sudden withdrawal → adrenal crisis (Addison’s-like collapse) → potentially fatal.

Addison’s Disease

Too little cortisol — the body running on empty

Addison’s = DEFICIENT Cortisol & Aldosterone

Think: NOT ENOUGH of everything.

Not enough sugar (hypoglycemia). Not enough Na/water retention (dehydration, hypotension).
Not enough immune modulation. The body can’t handle stress.

Addison’s — Causes

  • #1: Autoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex
  • Infections (TB, fungal), hemorrhage, tumors destroying adrenal tissue
  • Pituitary dysfunction → insufficient ACTH
  • Hypothalamic dysfunction → insufficient CRH
  • Abrupt withdrawal of exogenous steroids (adrenals have atrophied)
Fun fact: JFK had Addison’s disease — his famous tan was actually hyperpigmentation from excess ACTH stimulating melanocytes.

Addison’s — Manifestations High Yield

  • Hypotension (especially orthostatic/postural)
  • Hypoglycemia (no cortisol to raise glucose)
  • Hyperkalemia (no aldosterone to excrete K⁺) — HEART CONCERN
  • Hyponatremia (no aldosterone to retain Na⁺) — BRAIN CONCERN
  • Hyperpigmentation — dark/bronze skin (excess ACTH stimulates melanocytes)
  • Extreme fatigue, weakness, weight loss, salt craving, nausea, vomiting, chronic diarrhea, mood changes

Adrenal Crisis = Medical Emergency

Na⁺ drops, K⁺ spikes, BP crashes, kidneys shut down.

Profound fatigue, dehydration, vascular collapse.

Can be triggered by: stress, illness, surgery, or abruptly stopping steroids.

Addison’s — Treatment

  • Lifelong hormone replacement: hydrocortisone (cortisol) + fludrocortisone (aldosterone)
  • Wear medical alert bracelet at all times. Carry extra medication.
  • Dietary: INCREASE sodium intake (they lose Na⁺). LOW potassium diet (they retain K⁺). Fluid replacement.
  • Stress dosing: increase steroids during illness, surgery, or stress
Addison’s patients cannot mount a normal stress response. Any illness, injury, or surgery requires increased steroid doses — otherwise they risk adrenal crisis.
Quick Check

Addison’s Electrolytes

A patient with Addison’s disease would be expected to have which electrolyte pattern?
A. High sodium, low potassium
B. Low sodium, high potassium
C. High calcium, low phosphate
D. Normal electrolytes with elevated glucose
B. Low sodium, high potassium. Without aldosterone, the kidneys cannot retain Na⁺ (so it drops) and cannot excrete K⁺ (so it rises). High Na⁺/low K⁺ is the Cushing’s pattern. High calcium/low phosphate is hyperparathyroidism. Addison’s causes HYPOglycemia, not elevated glucose.

Cushing’s vs. Addison’s

Cushing’s (EXCESS Cortisol)

  • Cortisol: High
  • Glucose: High (steroid diabetes)
  • Na⁺: High (retained)
  • K⁺: Low (excreted)
  • BP: Hypertension
  • Weight: Obese (truncal)
  • Skin: Thin, bruises, striae
  • Look: Moon face, buffalo hump
  • Tx: Taper steroids, surgery

Addison’s (DEFICIENT Cortisol/Aldo)

  • Cortisol: Low
  • Glucose: Low
  • Na⁺: Low (lost)
  • K⁺: High (retained)
  • BP: Hypotension
  • Weight: Weight loss
  • Skin: Hyperpigmentation (bronze)
  • Look: Thin, fatigued
  • Tx: Lifelong replacement

Clinical Application

Put it all together
Clinical Scenario

Meet Rosa

Rosa, 45 y/o female, has been taking prednisone 40mg daily for 3 years for lupus. She presents with a round face, central obesity, purple stretch marks on her abdomen, blood glucose 245, and BP 162/98.

She asks if she can just stop the prednisone because of the side effects.

What is the diagnosis? Can she stop the prednisone?
Diagnosis: Iatrogenic Cushing’s syndrome.

Rosa has textbook Cushing’s from chronic exogenous steroid use: moon face, truncal obesity, purple striae, hyperglycemia, hypertension.

She absolutely CANNOT stop prednisone abruptly. After 3 years, her adrenal glands are completely suppressed. Sudden withdrawal → adrenal crisis → cardiovascular collapse → death. She must GRADUALLY taper under medical supervision while managing her complications.
Clinical Scenario

Meet James

James, 34 y/o male, presents to the ED with profound fatigue, BP 78/50, blood glucose 52, and dark bronze skin discoloration. Labs: Na⁺ 128, K⁺ 6.1. He reports salt cravings and has lost 20 lbs without trying.

What is the diagnosis? What is the most immediately dangerous lab value?
Diagnosis: Addison’s disease (adrenal insufficiency).

James has every hallmark: hypotension, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia (Na⁺ 128), hyperkalemia (K⁺ 6.1), bronze skin hyperpigmentation, weight loss, salt cravings, fatigue.

Most immediately dangerous: K⁺ 6.1 (hyperkalemia) — can cause fatal cardiac dysrhythmias. This is a medical emergency.

Treatment: IV fluids, IV hydrocortisone, continuous cardiac monitoring, correct electrolytes urgently.

Adrenal Disorders — The Big Picture

• Cushing’s = too much cortisol. Addison’s = not enough. They are OPPOSITES in every way.
• Cushing’s: high glucose, high Na⁺, low K⁺, hypertension, moon face, buffalo hump.
• Addison’s: low glucose, low Na⁺, high K⁺, hypotension, bronze skin, weight loss.
• NEVER abruptly stop steroids — gradual taper only.
• Adrenal crisis and Cushing’s complications can both be fatal.
• K⁺ = heart concern. Na⁺ = brain concern. Always check electrolytes.
