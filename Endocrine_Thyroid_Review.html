Thyroid Anatomy & Physiology

The gland that sets your metabolic speed

Thyroid Gland — Location & Structure

  • Located at base of neck, just below the larynx
  • Butterfly / H-shape — two lobes connected by a thin isthmus
  • Highly vascular — palpable on physical exam
  • Contains follicles that produce three key hormones
  • 4 parathyroid glands sit behind it (separate function — calcium regulation)

Three Thyroid Hormones

  • T4 (thyroxine) — regulates cellular metabolism & growth/development
  • T3 (triiodothyronine) — same function, but the more active form
  • T3/T4 account for ~95% of all thyroid hormones
  • Calcitonin — regulates calcium: inhibits osteoclasts (↓ Ca from bone), stimulates osteoblasts (↑ Ca into bone)
Key Point: Iodine is REQUIRED to synthesize T3 and T4. No iodine → hypothyroidism.

The Thyroid Axis (Negative Feedback)

Hypothalamus
releases TRH
→
Anterior Pituitary
releases TSH
→
Thyroid Gland
produces T3/T4
→
T3/T4 feedback
inhibits hypothalamus
When T3/T4 are sufficient, they signal back to STOP further TSH release. When they're low, TSH rises to stimulate more production.
Quick Check

Thyroid Axis

The hypothalamus senses low T3/T4 levels. What happens next?
A. Thyroid directly increases hormone production
B. Anterior pituitary releases TSH, which stimulates the thyroid to produce more T3/T4
C. Posterior pituitary releases ADH to compensate
D. Calcitonin is released to raise metabolism
B. Anterior pituitary releases TSH. The thyroid doesn't act on its own — it needs TSH from the anterior pituitary (triggered by TRH from the hypothalamus). ADH regulates water balance, not thyroid function. Calcitonin regulates calcium, not metabolism.

Goiter

The thyroid gets bigger — but why matters

Goiter — Visible Thyroid Enlargement

  • Can occur in BOTH hypo- and hyperthyroid states (and even normal function)
  • Iodine deficiency = #1 cause worldwide (rare in US due to iodized salt)
  • Autoimmune thyroid conditions are most common cause in the US
  • Usually painless but can compress trachea/esophagus → breathing & swallowing difficulty
  • Not necessarily malignant
Warning: Goiter does NOT tell you whether the thyroid is over- or underactive. You need lab values (TSH, T3/T4) to determine function.

Hypothyroidism

Everything slows down

Hypothyroidism = LOW Thyroid = The Body in Slow Motion

Not enough T3/T4. Metabolism drops. Everything runs cold, slow, and sluggish.

Hypothyroidism — Causes & Risk Factors

  • Most common cause: Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune — body attacks thyroid tissue)
  • Also iatrogenic: thyroid removal, radiation therapy
  • Can be congenital — all US newborns are screened at birth
  • Risk increases with advancing age
  • More common in women than men
  • Can result from hypothalamus or pituitary dysfunction (secondary hypothyroidism)

Hypothyroidism — Manifestations Think: SLOW

  • Fatigue, sluggishness, lethargy, depression
  • Cold intolerance — always cold
  • Weight gain despite decreased appetite
  • Bradycardia, hypotension
  • Constipation (↓ peristalsis)
  • Dry skin, brittle hair/nails, hair loss/thinning
  • Facial edema, hoarseness, large tongue
  • Hypercholesterolemia • Heavier menstrual periods
In infants: failure to grow, impaired mental development. This is why ALL newborns are screened.

Myxedema — Severe Hypothyroidism Emergency

  • Rare, life-threatening advanced hypothyroidism
  • Marked hypotension, respiratory depression
  • Hypothermia — body can't generate heat
  • Lethargy → coma
  • Cardiomegaly (enlarged heart)
Myxedema is a medical emergency. Vitals are LOW and SLOW across the board.

Hypothyroidism — Treatment

  • Lifelong thyroid hormone replacement therapy (Synthroid / levothyroxine)
  • Take on EMPTY stomach, 30 min before eating
  • Regular blood work to monitor and adjust dosage
  • Manage: weight, constipation, body temperature (avoid cold)
Key Concept: This is hormone REPLACEMENT — the thyroid isn't producing enough, so you supplement what's missing.
Quick Check

Hypothyroidism Presentation

A patient with hypothyroidism would most likely present with which set of findings?
A. Tachycardia, weight loss, heat intolerance
B. Bradycardia, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation
C. Exophthalmos, tremor, anxiety
D. Hypoglycemia, bronze skin, salt craving
B. Bradycardia, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation. Everything is SLOW in hypothyroidism. Options A and C describe HYPERthyroid/Graves' signs. Option D describes Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency).

Hyperthyroidism

Everything speeds up

Hyperthyroidism = TOO MUCH Thyroid = The Body in Overdrive

Excess T3/T4 creates a hypermetabolic state. Everything runs hot, fast, and wired.

Hyperthyroidism — Causes (Graves' Disease)

  • Most common cause: Graves' disease (autoimmune — antibodies overstimulate the thyroid)
  • Also: excessive iodine intake, thyroid tumors, thyroid inflammation
  • Excess thyroid hormone replacement (iatrogenic)
  • More common in women than men
Graves' = the thyroid is being told to GO GO GO by autoantibodies that mimic TSH.

Hyperthyroidism — Manifestations Think: FAST

  • Sudden weight loss despite increased appetite
  • Tachycardia, hypertension, palpitations
  • Heat intolerance, diaphoresis (sweating)
  • Nervousness, anxiety, irritability, insomnia
  • Fine hand tremors
  • Diarrhea (↑ peristalsis)
  • Exophthalmos (bulging eyes) — hallmark of Graves'
  • Goiter • Menstrual changes (lighter/absent periods)

Exophthalmos — The Graves' Hallmark

  • Bulging eyes — sclera visible around the iris
  • Eyes may not fully close → cornea dries out → scarring → vision loss
  • Manage with: cool compresses, sunglasses, eye lubricants, elevate HOB
Warning: Exophthalmos may NOT resolve even after hyperthyroidism is treated. Protect the corneas.

Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxicosis)

Medical Emergency
A sudden, life-threatening surge of T3/T4. Can be triggered by infection or stress.

Fever • Severely elevated BP • Tachycardia • Decreased mental alertness • Abdominal pain

Hyperthyroidism — Treatment

  • Goal: decrease thyroid hormone production
  • Radioactive iodine — destroys overactive thyroid tissue
  • Antithyroid agents (methimazole, PTU)
  • Beta blockers — control heart rate and tremors
  • Surgical removal (thyroidectomy) — patient may then need lifelong replacement
  • Increase caloric and calcium intake (hypermetabolism depletes both)
Key Point: After radioactive iodine or surgery, the patient often becomes HYPOthyroid and needs replacement therapy.
Quick Check

Hyperthyroidism Diagnosis

A patient presents with weight loss, tachycardia, tremors, heat intolerance, and bulging eyes. What is the most likely diagnosis?
A. Hypothyroidism / Hashimoto's
B. Cushing's disease
C. Hyperthyroidism / Graves' disease
D. Addison's disease
C. Hyperthyroidism / Graves' disease. Exophthalmos (bulging eyes) + hypermetabolic symptoms (weight loss, tachycardia, tremors, heat intolerance) = classic Graves'. Hashimoto's causes the opposite symptoms. Cushing's causes weight gain, moon face, buffalo hump. Addison's causes hypotension, hyperpigmentation, fatigue.

Hypothyroidism vs. Hyperthyroidism

Hypothyroidism (SLOW)

  • Metabolism: Slow
  • Weight: Gain
  • Temperature: Cold intolerant
  • Heart: Bradycardia, hypotension
  • GI: Constipation
  • Skin/Hair: Dry, brittle, coarse
  • Mood: Depressed, sluggish
  • Cause: Hashimoto's (autoimmune destroy)
  • Treatment: Hormone REPLACEMENT

Hyperthyroidism (FAST)

  • Metabolism: Fast
  • Weight: Loss
  • Temperature: Heat intolerant
  • Heart: Tachycardia, hypertension
  • GI: Diarrhea
  • Skin/Hair: Fine, thin, diaphoresis
  • Mood: Anxious, irritable, insomnia
  • Cause: Graves' (autoimmune overstimulate)
  • Treatment: SUPPRESS production

Clinical Application

Put it all together
Clinical Case 1

Meet Margaret, 58 y/o Female

Margaret presents with fatigue, a 15-lb weight gain over 6 months, reports being always cold, has constipation, dry skin, and thinning hair.

Labs: TSH elevated, T4 low.

What is the diagnosis and expected treatment?
A. Graves' disease — start antithyroid medication
B. Hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's) — lifelong levothyroxine replacement
C. Cushing's syndrome — reduce cortisol
D. Normal aging — no treatment needed
B. Hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's). Elevated TSH = pituitary is trying harder because T4 is low. All symptoms point to a slowed metabolism: weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, hair loss. Treatment: lifelong levothyroxine (Synthroid) replacement on an empty stomach. Graves' would show opposite symptoms. Cushing's causes a different pattern (moon face, buffalo hump). These findings together are pathologic, not normal aging.
Clinical Case 2

Meet Devon, 32 y/o Female

Devon has lost 20 lbs in 2 months despite eating constantly. Heart rate 112, BP 158/92, hands trembling. She reports feeling "wired and anxious all the time." Her eyes appear to be bulging.

What is the diagnosis? What is the most dangerous potential complication?
A. Panic disorder — prescribe anxiolytics
B. Hypothyroidism — start levothyroxine
C. Graves' disease (hyperthyroidism) — most dangerous complication: thyroid storm
D. Pheochromocytoma — surgical removal of adrenal tumor
C. Graves' disease (hyperthyroidism). Weight loss + tachycardia + tremors + anxiety + exophthalmos = classic Graves'. The most dangerous complication is thyroid storm (thyrotoxicosis) — a sudden surge of T3/T4 that can cause cardiovascular collapse and death. Panic disorder could mimic some symptoms, but doesn't explain weight loss + exophthalmos. Hypothyroidism is the opposite presentation. Pheochromocytoma has similar tachycardia/hypertension but no exophthalmos or weight loss pattern.

Thyroid Disorders — The Big Picture

• Thyroid sets the metabolic speed: T3/T4 via the hypothalamus-pituitary-thyroid axis.
• Hypo = everything SLOW (cold, tired, constipated, weight gain). Hyper = everything FAST (hot, wired, diarrhea, weight loss).
• Hashimoto's = autoimmune destruction (hypo). Graves' = autoimmune overstimulation (hyper).
• Goiter can occur in BOTH. Exophthalmos is specific to Graves'.
• Thyroid storm = medical emergency. Myxedema = medical emergency. Opposite ends, both deadly.
