Section A

Thyroid Anatomy & Physiology

The metabolic gas pedal of the body
Thyroid

Location & Structure

  • Butterfly-shaped gland in the anterior neck, just below the larynx
  • Two lobes connected by a central isthmus
  • Richly vascular — one of the most perfused organs in the body
  • Produces three hormones: T3, T4, and calcitonin
  • Iodine is required to make T3 and T4
Why iodine matters: no iodine, no T3/T4 → the gland enlarges (goiter) trying to compensate. US rarely sees this because of iodized salt.
Thyroid

The Three Thyroid Hormones

T4
Thyroxine (T4)
  • Regulates cellular metabolism
  • Supports growth & development
  • Converted to active T3 in tissues
T3
Triiodothyronine (T3)
  • The more active form
  • Same functions as T4
  • T3 + T4 = ~95% of thyroid output
Ca
Calcitonin
  • LOWERS blood calcium
  • Inhibits osteoclasts, stimulates osteoblasts → keeps Ca in bone
  • Opposite of PTH
Calci-TONE-in tones down calcium. Calcitonin is a thyroid hormone, but it’s a separate system from T3/T4 — different cells (parafollicular C-cells), different axis.

The HPT Axis

Hypothalamus(brain) — senses T3/T4, releases TRH
→
Anterior Pituitaryreleases TSH
→
Thyroid Gland(neck) — releases …
→
T3 / T4the hormones — circulate in blood, act on every tissue
↻ Negative Feedback (sensing)
Reading the labs: TSH and T3/T4 move in opposite directions in primary thyroid disease. Low T3/T4 → hypothalamus ramps up TRH → TSH rises. High T3/T4 → hypothalamus quiets TRH → TSH drops.
Quick Check
A patient’s labs show elevated TSH and low T4. How should you interpret this?
A. Primary hyperthyroidism — excess T3/T4 suppressing TSH
No — that’s the opposite pattern (low TSH, high T3/T4).
B. Primary hypothyroidism — thyroid underproducing, pituitary compensating
Correct! The thyroid is failing (low T4), so the pituitary ramps up TSH trying to stimulate it. Negative feedback in action.
C. Secondary hypothyroidism — pituitary failure
No. In secondary (pituitary) hypothyroidism, BOTH TSH and T4 would be low. Here TSH is high.
D. Normal thyroid function
No. Elevated TSH with low T4 is always pathologic — the body is trying to make more hormone and failing.
Section B

Goiter

The thyroid gets bigger — but why matters
Goiter

Visible Thyroid Enlargement

  • Can occur in BOTH hypo- and hyperthyroid states (and even normal function)
  • Iodine deficiency = #1 cause worldwide (rare in US due to iodized salt)
  • Autoimmune thyroid conditions are the most common cause in the US
  • Usually painless but can compress trachea/esophagus → breathing & swallowing difficulty
  • Not necessarily malignant
Goiter does NOT tell you function. You need lab values (TSH, T3/T4) to determine whether the thyroid is over- or underactive.
Section C

Hypothyroidism

Everything slows down
Core Concept

Hypothyroidism = Everything SLOW

Not enough T3/T4. Metabolism drops. The body runs cold, sluggish, and slow in every system.
Hypothyroidism

In infants: failure to grow, impaired mental development. This is why ALL US newborns are screened.
Hypothyroidism

Treatment — Hormone Replacement

  • Lifelong levothyroxine (Synthroid) — synthetic T4
  • Take on EMPTY stomach, 30 min before eating — food blocks absorption
  • Start low and titrate up based on TSH recheck (4–6 weeks)
  • Monitor for signs of over-replacement (palpitations, weight loss, insomnia)
Patient teaching: never skip doses or stop abruptly — symptoms return gradually and myxedema risk rises.
Emergency

Myxedema = Severe Hypothyroidism

Vitals low and slow across the board: marked hypotension, respiratory depression, hypothermia (can’t generate heat), lethargy → coma, cardiomegaly.
Quick Check
A 58-year-old woman reports 15-lb weight gain, fatigue, constipation, dry skin, and feeling cold all the time. Which lab pattern do you expect?
A. TSH high, T4 low
Correct! Classic primary hypothyroidism. Thyroid is failing (low T4), pituitary compensates (high TSH).
B. TSH low, T4 high
No — that’s the hyperthyroid pattern. Symptoms would be opposite (weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia).
C. TSH normal, T4 normal
No. A symptomatic patient with this constellation almost certainly has abnormal labs.
D. TSH low, T4 low
That would suggest secondary (pituitary) hypothyroidism — less common than Hashimoto’s.
Section D

Hyperthyroidism

Everything speeds up
Core Concept

Hyperthyroidism = Everything FAST

Excess T3/T4 creates a hypermetabolic state. Every system runs hot, fast, and wired.

Hyperthyroidism

Exophthalmos — The Graves’ Hallmark

  • Bulging eyes — sclera visible around the iris
  • Eyes may not fully close → cornea dries → scarring → vision loss
  • Manage with: cool compresses, sunglasses, eye lubricants, elevate HOB
Exophthalmos may NOT resolve even after hyperthyroidism is treated. Protect the corneas.
Hyperthyroidism

Treatment — Suppress Production

☢
Radioactive IodineDestroys overactive thyroid tissue
💊
Antithyroid AgentsMethimazole, PTU
♥
Beta BlockersControl HR & tremors (symptom management)
💉
ThyroidectomySurgical removal — may need lifelong replacement
After radioactive iodine or surgery, patients often become HYPOthyroid and need levothyroxine. Also increase caloric & calcium intake during hypermetabolic phase.
Emergency

Thyroid Storm = Thyrotoxicosis

Sudden, life-threatening surge of T3/T4 — often triggered by infection or stress. Fever · severely elevated BP · tachycardia · decreased mental alertness · abdominal pain.
Quick Check
A patient presents with weight loss, HR 112 (normal 60–100), tremors, heat intolerance, and bulging eyes. What is the most likely diagnosis?
A. Hypothyroidism / Hashimoto’s
Opposite symptoms. Hypothyroid = weight gain, bradycardia, cold intolerance.
B. Cushing’s syndrome
No. Cushing’s causes weight GAIN, moon face, buffalo hump — and no exophthalmos.
C. Hyperthyroidism / Graves’ disease
Correct! Exophthalmos + hypermetabolic symptoms (weight loss, tachycardia, tremors, heat intolerance) = classic Graves’.
D. Addison’s disease
No. Addison’s causes hypotension, hyperpigmentation, fatigue — not tachycardia or exophthalmos.

Hypothyroidism vs. Hyperthyroidism

Hypothyroidism (SLOW)

  • Metabolism: Slow
  • Weight: Gain
  • Temperature: Cold intolerant
  • Heart: Bradycardia, hypotension
  • GI: Constipation
  • Skin/Hair: Dry, brittle, coarse
  • Mood: Depressed, sluggish
  • Cause: Hashimoto’s (autoimmune destroy)
  • Labs: ↑ TSH, ↓ T4
  • Tx: REPLACE — levothyroxine

Hyperthyroidism (FAST)

  • Metabolism: Fast
  • Weight: Loss
  • Temperature: Heat intolerant
  • Heart: Tachycardia, hypertension
  • GI: Diarrhea
  • Skin/Hair: Fine, thin, diaphoresis
  • Mood: Anxious, irritable, insomnia
  • Cause: Graves’ (autoimmune overstimulate)
  • Labs: ↓ TSH, ↑ T3/T4
  • Tx: SUPPRESS — methimazole, RAI, surgery
Section E

Clinical Application

Put it all together
Clinical Scenario
Margaret, 58F, presents with fatigue, 15-lb weight gain over 6 months, reports being always cold, constipation, dry skin, and thinning hair. Labs: TSH elevated (normal 0.4–4.0), T4 low (normal 4.5–12.0).
What is the diagnosis and expected treatment?
A. Graves’ disease — start antithyroid medication
No. Graves’ causes opposite symptoms: weight loss, tachycardia, exophthalmos.
B. Hashimoto’s hypothyroidism — lifelong levothyroxine
Correct! Elevated TSH + low T4 + slowed-metabolism symptoms = classic Hashimoto’s. Treatment: lifelong levothyroxine on an empty stomach.
C. Cushing’s syndrome — reduce cortisol
No. Cushing’s has different pattern: moon face, buffalo hump, hyperglycemia, HTN.
D. Normal aging — no treatment needed
No. These findings together are pathologic. The TSH/T4 pattern alone confirms disease.
Clinical Scenario
Devon, 32F, has lost 20 lbs in 2 months despite eating constantly. HR 112 (normal 60–100), BP 158/92 (HTN stage 2), hands trembling. She reports feeling “wired and anxious all the time.” Her eyes appear bulging.
What is the diagnosis, and what is the most dangerous complication?
A. Panic disorder — prescribe anxiolytics
Anxiety could mimic some symptoms but doesn’t explain weight loss + tachycardia + exophthalmos. Missing the diagnosis would miss thyroid storm risk.
B. Hypothyroidism — start levothyroxine
Wrong direction. Levothyroxine on a hyperthyroid patient could trigger thyroid storm.
C. Graves’ disease — thyroid storm is the deadliest complication
Correct! Weight loss + tachycardia + tremors + anxiety + exophthalmos = classic Graves’. Thyroid storm (thyrotoxicosis) is a sudden T3/T4 surge that can cause cardiovascular collapse and death. Often triggered by infection or stress.
D. Pheochromocytoma — surgical removal of adrenal tumor
Pheo can mimic tachycardia/HTN but no exophthalmos or weight-loss pattern. Graves’ is far more common.
Big Picture

Thyroid Disorders — The Big Picture

• Thyroid sets the metabolic pace via T3/T4 through the HPT axis.
• Hypo = SLOW (cold, tired, constipated, weight gain). Hyper = FAST (hot, wired, diarrhea, weight loss).
• Hashimoto’s = autoimmune destruction (hypo). Graves’ = autoimmune overstimulation (hyper).
• Labs move OPPOSITE in primary disease: ↑ TSH + ↓ T4 = hypo; ↓ TSH + ↑ T3/T4 = hyper.
• Goiter occurs in BOTH. Exophthalmos is specific to Graves’.
• Thyroid storm & myxedema = medical emergencies. Opposite ends, both deadly.
