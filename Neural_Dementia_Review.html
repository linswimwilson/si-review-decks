Quick Anchor

Dementia lives in the cortex — the outer layer where higher-order thinking happens. Memory, language, judgment, personality — all cortical jobs. The prefrontal lobe handles behavior and impulse control. If you need a refresh on lobes and what each does, skim the Foundations deck first. This deck builds on that map.

Section A

What’s Dementia?

Cortical function loss — and what causes it
Core Concept

Dementia = ↓ Cortical Function

Impaired cognition + motor function. Maybe memory. When behavior/personality changes show up — the prefrontal lobe is involved.
What Breaks Down

Dementia Hits the Cortex

🧠
Cognition
  • Thinking, reasoning, language
  • Can’t follow conversations
  • Judgment fails over time
🕵
Motor Function
  • Gait changes, balance
  • Loss of fine motor skills
  • Eventually loss of ADLs
M
Memory
  • Short-term hit first
  • Long-term preserved until late
  • Not always the first symptom
💪
Behavior / Personality
  • Prefrontal lobe involvement
  • Labile emotions, aggression
  • Loss of impulse control

Quick Check
Which set of risk factors overlaps with cardiovascular disease and makes vascular dementia particularly concerning?
A. Age, female sex, low education
These are dementia risk factors, but they aren’t the CVD-shared set. Look for modifiable vascular risks.
B. HTN, ↑ cholesterol, diabetes
Correct. Atherosclerosis → altered cerebral blood flow → vascular dementia. These are the same modifiable risks that drive CAD/stroke (spiral Ch. 4).
C. Family history and TBI only
Both are risk factors, but neither is shared with CVD. Genetics and trauma don’t overlap with vascular risk.
D. AIDS and chronic toxin exposure
Infections/toxins cause dementia but aren’t CVD risk factors.
Section B

Alzheimer’s Disease

The most common dementia — slow, insidious, no cure
Frequency Anchor

50–80% of All Dementia is Alzheimer’s

Insidious onset — 10 to 20 years of slow decline. Often starts in 50s–60s. Early exam looks normal; patient forgets recent events. Late stages: behavior changes, aggression.
Physical Brain Changes

What You’d See on CT / Autopsy

β
Beta-Amyloid Plaques
  • Protein clumps between neurons
  • Block communication
τ
Neurofibrillary Tangles
  • Twisted tau protein inside neurons
  • Neuron dies
⚡
Loss of Neural Connections
  • Synapses fail — signals don’t travel
  • Cortex & mid-brain affected
CT
CT Findings
  • Brain atrophy (shrinkage)
  • Enlarged ventricles
  • Change in cortex → ↓ memory
All three pathologies together → atrophy & degeneration. Memory loss is a downstream result of losing cortical tissue and connections.
Quick Check
A CT scan on a 74F with suspected Alzheimer’s would MOST likely show:
A. A focal hemorrhage in the frontal lobe
That’s a hemorrhagic stroke pattern, not Alzheimer’s. Alzheimer’s is diffuse atrophy, not a bleed.
B. Normal brain tissue with no abnormalities
Early in the disease this can look normal, but by the time dementia is clinically suspected, atrophy is usually visible.
C. Brain atrophy with enlarged ventricles
Correct. Loss of cortical tissue → brain shrinks → the CSF spaces (ventricles) expand to fill the room. Classic CT appearance in advanced Alzheimer’s.
D. A midline shift with effacement of sulci
That’s a mass effect/↑ICP picture. Alzheimer’s is the opposite — tissue loss, not expansion.
Section C

Progression

The order symptoms appear — a true temporal cascade
Alzheimer’s Progression

The Cascade of Loss — order matters

1
Loss of abstract thought — exam may still look normal
2
Can’t follow conversations — family notices first
3
Disorientation — time first, then place
4
Loss of judgment — stove on, wanders, unsafe
5
Loss of continence
6
Personality change — prefrontal involvement
7
Aggression
8
Hallucinations — cortical function nearly gone
Quick Check
A family says “Mom started forgetting names a year ago, now she can’t follow conversations. What’s likely next?”
A. Aggression and hallucinations
These are later-stage findings. She’s still early. Several steps need to come first.
B. Disorientation to time and place
Correct. Progression is: memory → conversations → disorientation → judgment → continence → personality → aggression → hallucinations. She’s at the “conversations” step — disorientation is next.
C. Loss of continence
Continence loss comes later, after judgment fails.
D. Personality change
Personality change is late — after disorientation, judgment loss, and continence loss.
Section D

Other Dementias & Treatment

Lewy body — and what we can (and can’t) do about any of them

Alzheimer’s vs Lewy Body

Alzheimer’s

  • 50–80% of all dementia
  • Insidious — 10 to 20 years
  • Memory loss dominates early
  • β-amyloid plaques + neurofibrillary tangles
  • Atrophy + enlarged ventricles on CT
  • Steady, predictable decline

Lewy Body Dementia

  • Slow but progressive
  • Fluctuation in cognition (good days + bad days)
  • Hallucinations — often early and prominent
  • Motor gait imbalance
  • Lewy body protein deposits
Key differentiator: if you’re seeing fluctuation + hallucinations + motor gait — think Lewy body, not Alzheimer’s. Hallucinations in Alzheimer’s come late; in Lewy body they come early.
Treatment

No Cure — Only Slow the Decline

Rx
Cholinesterase Inhibitors
  • Slows acetylcholine breakdown
  • Slows progression, doesn’t reverse
E
Vitamin E
  • Antioxidant support
  • May slow cellular damage
🌱
Ginkgo
  • Herbal supplement
  • May support cognition
There is no cure. Every treatment we have only slows progression. That’s why family teaching and safety planning matter so much — that’s where we actually change outcomes.
Section E

Safety & Family Teaching

Where nursing makes the biggest difference
Care Anchor

“What is meaningful to them?”

The guiding question for every decision. Use what they care about — familiar TV shows, a beloved watch, photos of grandkids, a calendar routine — as environmental clues to identity. Cognitive stimulation works best when it’s personal.
Environmental Safety

Keep Them Safe at Home

🚗
Disconnect the Car Battery
  • They may try to drive
  • Removes the option entirely
🧻
Remove Throw Rugs
  • Motor changes → fall risk
  • One of the simplest wins
🔥
Turn Off the Gas
  • Stove left on is a top danger
  • Judgment loss = fire risk
👪
Supervision + Help with Meds
  • Multi-generational household helps
  • Keep environment calm
📝
Orientation Clues & Routine
  • Calendar, familiar TV, newspaper
  • Clues about identity — watch, photos of loved ones
  • Remind to eat · physical activity · advanced planning
Cognitive Stimulation

Keep the Brain Engaged

📝
Crosswords
  • Language + recall
🎱
Match Games
  • Short-term memory exercise
🧩
Puzzles
  • Visuospatial + patience
🎨
Crafts
  • Fine motor + creativity
📷
Photos of Loved Ones
  • Identity + connection
🚶
Physical Activity
  • Prevents immobility complications
Complications of prolonged immobility: infections, falls, malnutrition/anorexia, dehydration. Keep moving — body AND brain.
Section F

Clinical Application

Mrs. Garcia, 74F — put it together
Case Study
Mrs. Garcia, 74F, brought by her daughter. 2-year progressive memory loss — now getting lost in her neighborhood, leaving the stove on, recently hit her husband (never aggressive before).

Exam: disoriented to time + place · poor short-term memory, long-term intact · ADL difficulty, occasional incontinence.
CT: brain atrophy, enlarged ventricles.
Risks: age 74, female, mother had Alzheimer’s, HTN.
Given the stove incidents and wandering, what is the nurse’s most important teaching priority for the family?
A. Reassure the family that cholinesterase inhibitors will reverse the decline
Incorrect on two counts. There is no cure — meds only slow progression. And reassurance about reversal is false hope.
B. Environmental safety — disconnect the car battery, turn off gas, supervision, remove fall hazards
Correct. Judgment loss + wandering + stove on = real safety risks right now. Nursing can’t cure Alzheimer’s, but we CAN prevent the fire, the fall, and the car accident through environmental teaching. Most important priority.
C. Encourage the family to place her in a memory care facility this week
Too soon and not our call. Advanced planning matters, but forcing placement isn’t priority teaching — safety in her current environment is.
D. Increase her cognitive stimulation activities to prevent the aggression
Cognitive stimulation is part of care, but aggression is progression of the disease, not something puzzles fix. Safety is the immediate priority.
Big Picture

Dementia — The Big Picture

• Dementia = ↓ cortical function. Cognition + motor. Behavior/personality → prefrontal.
• Etiologies: atherosclerosis, infections/toxins (incl. AIDS), genetic, unknown.
• Alzheimer’s = 50–80% of all dementia. Insidious 10–20 yr decline. β-amyloid + tangles + lost connections → atrophy + enlarged ventricles.
• Progression: memory → conversations → orientation → judgment → continence → personality → aggression → hallucinations.
• Lewy body = fluctuation + hallucinations + motor gait. Hallucinations early, not late.
• No cure. Cholinesterase inhibitors, Vit E, ginkgo slow progression only.
• Safety: disconnect car battery, remove throw rugs, turn off gas, supervision.
• “What is meaningful to them?” — the care anchor.
