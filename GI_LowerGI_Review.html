Lower GI Anatomy

Where water gets absorbed and waste takes shape

The Large Bowel Path

  • Ileocecal valve = gateway from small intestine → large intestine. Waste is very liquid here.
  • Ascending colon (right side) — liquid stool
  • Hepatic flexure → Transverse colon — toothpaste consistency
  • Splenic flexure → Descending colon (left side) — becoming solid
  • Sigmoid (S-curve) → Rectum → Anus
  • Primary job: absorb water. Normal transit = 36–48 hrs. Brown color from bile pigment.

Clinical Landmarks to Know

  • Appendix attached to cecum — lower RIGHT quadrant
  • Pain lower RIGHT = could be appendix or small bowel (ileocecal area)
  • Pain lower LEFT = large intestine (sigmoid/descending)
  • Can live without any part of large bowel (ostomy) — but liquid stool + need more water
  • Need minimum 12 inches of small bowel to survive — otherwise IV nutrition (TPN)
  • Gas = methane, a normal by-product of digestion
Quick Check

Lower GI Anatomy

A patient has pain in the lower LEFT quadrant. Which part of the GI tract is most likely involved?
A. Appendix
B. Ascending colon
C. Sigmoid / Descending colon
D. Ileocecal valve
C. Sigmoid / Descending colon. Lower LEFT quadrant pain maps to the sigmoid and descending colon. The appendix and ileocecal valve are lower RIGHT. The ascending colon is on the right side.

Diarrhea

When transit is too fast, water can't be absorbed

Too Fast = Water Not Absorbed

Content moves through the large bowel too quickly for water reabsorption. Result: watery stool, electrolyte loss, and dehydration.

Common Causes of Diarrhea

  • Viruses & bacteria — NOT influenza (flu is respiratory!)
  • Certain foods: high fat + sugar move fast = "dumping syndrome"
  • Excess sugar → hyperosmolar → pulls water into GI lumen → diarrhea
  • Laxative overuse
  • Inflammatory bowel disease — Crohn's (small bowel) vs UC (large bowel)

What You Lose With Diarrhea High Yield

  • You lose: Water, K+ (potassium), HCO3− (bicarbonate)
  • Result: Metabolic ACIDOSIS with LOW potassium
  • Exception — Usually acidosis pairs with HIGH K+, but diarrhea = acidosis + LOW K+
  • Low K+ → concerned about the heart (dysrhythmias)
  • Young children + very old = earliest onset of dehydration

Stool Color Guide

Bright Red (Frank Blood) Lower GI bleed — rectum/sigmoid. Active bleeding.
Maroon Upper descending / transverse bleed. Blood has been in GI a while.
Black / Tarry (Melena) Upper GI (stomach/duodenum). Fully digested blood. Terrible odor.
Clay / Pale Liver problem — no bile pigment reaching stool.
Occult (Hidden Blood) Not visible — found by lab testing. Could indicate bowel cancer or slow bleed. "Straining" to evacuate is NOT normal.
Quick Check

Diarrhea & Acid-Base

A patient with chronic diarrhea has a pH of 7.29. What acid-base imbalance is present, and why?
A. Metabolic alkalosis from K+ loss
B. Metabolic acidosis from HCO3− loss
C. Respiratory acidosis from hypoventilation
D. Metabolic alkalosis from HCl loss
B. Metabolic acidosis from HCO3− loss. pH 7.29 is acidotic (below 7.35). Diarrhea flushes out bicarbonate (the body's base buffer), causing metabolic acidosis. Remember: diarrhea = acidosis + low K+ (the exception to the usual acidosis-high K+ pairing). HCl loss from vomiting causes alkalosis, not acidosis.

Diarrhea — Treatment

  • Find the cause — infection? medication? diet?
  • Fasting from solid food initially; advance to bland diet
  • High-electrolyte fluids (oral rehydration solutions)
  • Antidiarrheals (OTC or Rx) as appropriate
  • Monitor K+ and fluid balance closely
  • Dietary fiber prevents diarrhea AND constipation (whole grains, oatmeal, fruit/veggie peels, beans, bran)

Constipation

When transit is too slow, too much water is absorbed

Too Slow = Too Much Water Absorbed

Stool sits in the colon too long. Water keeps getting pulled out. Result: hard, dry stool that's difficult to pass.

Constipation — Causes

  • Medications that slow peristalsis: opioids, iron, calcium channel blockers
  • Sedentary lifestyle / ignoring urge to defecate
  • Laxative overuse → bowel dependency
  • Low fiber, low fluid intake
  • Colon cancer can physically block transit
  • Eating non-food items (pica) → constipation / obstruction

Complications & Treatment

  • Fissures from straining (tears in anal tissue)
  • Hemorrhoids — bulging veins that itch + bleed
  • Bowel sounds: hypoactive (sluggish gut)

Treatment Ladder

  • ↑ Fiber, ↑ Water, ↑ Activity
  • Stool softeners (hold water in stool)
  • Laxatives → Enema → Digital removal (last resort)
Quick Check

Diarrhea vs. Constipation

A post-op patient on opioid pain management reports no bowel movement for 4 days. Bowel sounds are hypoactive. Which intervention is MOST appropriate to try first?
A. Administer an enema
B. Increase fluids, fiber, and encourage ambulation
C. Digital removal of stool
D. Administer antidiarrheals
B. Increase fluids, fiber, and encourage ambulation. Opioids slow peristalsis, causing constipation. Start with the least invasive interventions first: fluids, fiber, and movement. Enema and digital removal are later steps. Antidiarrheals would make it worse!

Diverticular Disease

Quiet pouches vs. angry pouches — know the difference

Diverticulosis vs. Diverticulitis

Diverticulosis (Quiet)

  • Pouches PRESENT but asymptomatic
  • Common in adults > 60
  • Usually an incidental finding
  • Caused by low fiber + increased intraluminal pressure

Diverticulitis (Inflamed)

  • Pouches INFLAMED / INFECTED
  • Feces trapped → rots → inflammation
  • LLQ pain, fever, ↑WBC, blood in stool
  • Can PERFORATE → peritonitis (fatal) or hemorrhage → shock

Diverticulitis — What to Recognize

  • LLQ tenderness (sigmoid colon territory)
  • Low-grade fever, ↑ WBC
  • Blood in stool, abdominal distension
  • CT abdomen = gold standard for diagnosis
  • NOT colonoscopy during acute episode — perforation risk! Scope AFTER resolution.

Diverticulitis — Treatment

  • Acute: bowel rest (NPO), IV fluids, IV antibiotics — 3–6 days, often hospitalized
  • Prevention: high fiber, hydration, avoid prolonged sitting on toilet
  • Recurrent: surgical removal of segment → possible ostomy
  • Emergency Rigid abdomen + rebound tenderness + fever spike = perforation → surgical emergency
Clinical Scenario

Meet Dorothy

Dorothy, 68 years old, presents to the ED with LLQ pain that began 2 days ago and has worsened. She has a low-grade fever of 38.8°C, WBC 14,200, and reports blood-tinged stool.

CT abdomen reveals inflamed pouches in the sigmoid colon.

What is the diagnosis? What is the key concern you should watch for?
Diagnosis: Acute Diverticulitis.

Dorothy has classic signs: LLQ pain (sigmoid territory), low-grade fever, elevated WBC (infection), blood-tinged stool, and CT-confirmed inflamed sigmoid pouches.

Key concern: Perforation. If those inflamed pouches rupture, intestinal contents spill into the peritoneal cavity → peritonitis (potentially fatal). Watch for: rigid abdomen, rebound tenderness, sudden fever spike, hemodynamic instability.

Expected management: NPO, IV fluids, IV antibiotics, close monitoring.

Lower GI — The Big Picture

• Large intestine's job = absorb water. Transit time is everything.
• Too fast (diarrhea) = metabolic acidosis + low K+
• Too slow (constipation) = hard stool, straining, fissures, hemorrhoids
• Diverticulosis = quiet pouches. Diverticulitis = infected → perforation risk
• Vomiting = metabolic alkalosis. Diarrhea = metabolic acidosis. Know the difference.
