Section A

Lower GI Anatomy

The large bowel’s job is to absorb water — transit time is everything
Anatomy

The Large Bowel Path — Follow the Stool

Primary job = absorb water. Content enters as liquid at the ileocecal valve and exits as solid stool. Too fast = diarrhea (not enough time to absorb). Too slow = constipation (absorbs too much).
Anatomy

Clinical Landmarks

12″
Small Bowel Minimum
  • Need ≥ 12 inches to survive
  • Less → TPN (IV nutrition)
R
Lower RIGHT Quadrant
  • Appendix (attached to cecum)
  • Ileocecal valve / small bowel junction
  • Pain → think appendicitis
L
Lower LEFT Quadrant
  • Sigmoid / descending colon
  • Pain → think diverticulitis
⚖
Large Bowel Is Optional
  • Can live without any part (ostomy)
  • Tradeoff: liquid stool, more water needs
Quick Check
A patient has pain in the lower LEFT quadrant. Which part of the GI tract is most likely involved?
A. Appendix
No — appendix is lower RIGHT (attached to cecum).
B. Ascending colon
No — ascending colon is on the right side.
C. Sigmoid / descending colon
Correct! Lower LEFT pain maps to the sigmoid and descending colon — and is the classic location of diverticulitis.
D. Ileocecal valve
No — the ileocecal valve is in the lower RIGHT.
Section B

Diarrhea

When transit is too fast, water can’t be absorbed
Core Concept

Too Fast = Water Not Absorbed

Content moves through the large bowel too quickly for water reabsorption. Result: watery stool, electrolyte loss, and dehydration.
Diarrhea

Common Causes

🦠
Infection
  • Viruses & bacteria
  • NOT influenza (flu is respiratory!)
🍲
Dietary
  • High fat + sugar → “dumping”
  • Excess sugar → hyperosmolar pull
💊
Medications
  • Laxative overuse
  • Some antibiotics (C. diff risk)
IBD
Inflammatory Bowel
  • Crohn’s (small bowel)
  • Ulcerative colitis (large bowel)
Diarrhea

What You Lose High Yield

H₂O
Water
  • Dehydration
  • Kids & elderly dehydrate fastest
K⁺
Potassium
  • Hypokalemia
  • Cardiac dysrhythmia risk
HCO₃
Bicarbonate
  • Lose the body’s base buffer
  • → Metabolic acidosis
!
The Exception
  • Usually: acidosis + HIGH K⁺
  • Diarrhea: acidosis + LOW K⁺
Remember the pair: diarrhea = metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia. Vomiting = metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia (lost HCl and K⁺).
Diarrhea / GI Bleed

Stool Color Guide — Where Is the Bleed?

Clay / Pale — LiverNo bile pigment reaching stool. Liver can’t process or release bile.
Black / Tarry (Melena) — Upper GIStomach / duodenum bleed. Fully digested blood. Terrible odor.
Maroon — Mid ColonUpper descending / transverse bleed. Blood has been in GI a while.
Bright Red (Frank Blood) — Lower GIRectum / sigmoid bleed. Active bleeding, no time to digest.
Occult (Hidden Blood) — AnywhereNot visible — found by lab testing. Could indicate bowel cancer or slow bleed. Straining to evacuate is NOT normal.
Quick Check
A patient with chronic diarrhea has a pH of 7.29 (normal 7.35–7.45). What acid-base imbalance is present, and why?
A. Metabolic alkalosis from K⁺ loss
No — pH 7.29 is acidotic, not alkalotic. K⁺ loss itself doesn’t change pH this way.
B. Metabolic acidosis from HCO₃− loss
Correct! pH 7.29 is acidotic. Diarrhea flushes out bicarbonate — the body’s base buffer. Expect hypokalemia too (the exception to the usual acidosis + high K⁺ pairing).
C. Respiratory acidosis from hypoventilation
No — the source is GI (bicarb loss), not respiratory. Respiratory acidosis would be driven by CO₂ retention.
D. Metabolic alkalosis from HCl loss
That’s what vomiting causes (lost stomach acid). Diarrhea does the opposite — lose base, end up acidotic.
Diarrhea

Treatment — Find & Replace

  • Find the cause — infection? medication? diet?
  • Fasting from solid food initially; advance to bland diet
  • High-electrolyte fluids (oral rehydration solutions)
  • Antidiarrheals (OTC or Rx) as appropriate
  • Monitor K⁺ and fluid balance closely
  • Dietary fiber prevents BOTH diarrhea and constipation (whole grains, oatmeal, fruit/veggie peels, beans, bran)
Section C

Constipation

When transit is too slow, too much water is absorbed
Core Concept

Too Slow = Too Much Water Absorbed

Stool sits in the colon too long. Water keeps getting pulled out. Result: hard, dry stool that’s difficult to pass.
Constipation

Common Causes

💊
Medications
  • Opioids
  • Iron, calcium channel blockers
👪
Lifestyle
  • Sedentary / ignoring urge
  • Low fiber, low fluid
⚠
Mechanical
  • Colon cancer (physical block)
  • Pica (non-food items)
💥
Iatrogenic
  • Laxative overuse → dependency
  • Bowel stops working on its own

Complications & Treatment Ladder

Complications

  • Anal fissures from straining (tears in anal tissue)
  • Hemorrhoids — bulging veins that itch and bleed
  • Hypoactive bowel sounds (sluggish gut)
  • Fecal impaction if severe

Treatment Ladder (least → most invasive)

  • 1. ↑ Fiber, ↑ Water, ↑ Activity
  • 2. Stool softeners (hold water in stool)
  • 3. Laxatives
  • 4. Enema
  • 5. Digital removal (last resort)
Quick Check
A post-op patient on opioid pain management reports no bowel movement for 4 days. Bowel sounds are hypoactive. Which intervention is MOST appropriate to try FIRST?
A. Administer an enema
Too invasive for first-line. Enema is step 4 on the ladder — try less invasive options first.
B. Increase fluids, fiber, and encourage ambulation
Correct! Opioids slow peristalsis, causing constipation. Always start with the least invasive intervention: fluids, fiber, movement.
C. Digital removal of stool
Last resort only. Skip it as a first step.
D. Administer antidiarrheals
Opposite direction — that would make constipation worse.
Section D

Diverticular Disease

Quiet pouches vs. angry pouches — know the difference

Diverticulosis vs. Diverticulitis

Diverticulosis (Quiet)

  • Pouches PRESENT but asymptomatic
  • Common in adults > 60
  • Usually an incidental finding
  • Caused by low fiber + chronic straining

Diverticulitis (Inflamed)

  • Pouches INFLAMED / INFECTED
  • Feces trapped → rots → inflammation
  • LLQ pain, fever, ↑ WBC, blood in stool
  • Can PERFORATE → peritonitis or hemorrhage

Diverticulitis

What to Recognize

  • LLQ tenderness (sigmoid colon territory)
  • Low-grade fever, ↑ WBC
  • Blood in stool, abdominal distension
  • CT abdomen = gold standard for diagnosis
  • NOT colonoscopy during acute episode — perforation risk. Scope AFTER resolution.
Diverticulitis

Treatment — Rest & Antibiotics

  • Acute: bowel rest (NPO), IV fluids, IV antibiotics — 3–6 days, often hospitalized
  • Prevention: high fiber, hydration, avoid prolonged sitting on toilet
  • Recurrent: surgical removal of segment → possible ostomy
Emergency signs: rigid abdomen + rebound tenderness + fever spike = perforation → surgical emergency. Peritonitis is potentially fatal.
Clinical Scenario
Dorothy, 68F, presents to the ED with LLQ pain that began 2 days ago and has worsened. Temp 38.8°C (normal 36–37.5; low-grade fever), WBC 14,200 (normal 4,500–11,000 — elevated), and blood-tinged stool. CT abdomen reveals inflamed pouches in the sigmoid colon.
What is the diagnosis, and what is the key concern to watch for?
A. Diverticulosis — monitor at home
Diverticulosis is the quiet, asymptomatic form. Fever + elevated WBC + inflamed pouches on CT = diverticulitis (inflamed).
B. Acute diverticulitis — watch for perforation
Correct! Textbook diverticulitis: LLQ pain, low-grade fever, elevated WBC, blood-tinged stool, CT-confirmed inflamed sigmoid pouches. Perforation is the key concern → peritonitis (potentially fatal). Watch for rigid abdomen, rebound tenderness, sudden fever spike. Management: NPO, IV fluids, IV antibiotics.
C. Acute appendicitis — prep for surgery
Appendicitis is lower RIGHT quadrant (appendix is attached to the cecum on the right). Dorothy’s pain is LLQ = sigmoid territory.
D. Colon cancer — schedule colonoscopy now
Colonoscopy during ACUTE diverticulitis risks perforation. The CT finding of inflamed pouches, plus fever + elevated WBC, make diverticulitis the diagnosis. Scope later, after the acute episode resolves.
Big Picture

Lower GI — The Big Picture

• Large intestine’s job = absorb water. Transit time is everything.
• Too fast (diarrhea) = metabolic acidosis + low K⁺ (the exception).
• Too slow (constipation) = hard stool, straining, fissures, hemorrhoids.
• Diverticulosis = quiet pouches. Diverticulitis = infected → perforation risk.
• Vomiting = metabolic alkalosis. Diarrhea = metabolic acidosis. Know the difference.
