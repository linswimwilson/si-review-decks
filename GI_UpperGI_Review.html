Three sphincters to know: LES (stomach entrance), Pyloric (stomach exit), Ileocecal valve (small → large intestine)
Clinical Pearl

Can't speak? Can't swallow.

The pharynx controls both speech and swallowing. If a patient can't vocalize, assume aspiration risk and hold oral intake.
Quick Check
Which sphincter acts as the "gate" between the esophagus and stomach?
A. Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES)
Correct! The LES is the gate at the bottom of the esophagus. When it fails, acid goes UP (GERD).
B. Pyloric Sphincter
Not quite. The pyloric sphincter is the EXIT gate of the stomach, controlling flow into the duodenum.
C. Ileocecal Valve
No — the ileocecal valve is much lower, between the small and large intestine.
D. Upper Esophageal Sphincter
The UES is at the top of the esophagus near the pharynx. It's not the gate that prevents acid reflux.
Section B

Vomiting

Protective reflex, warning signs, and what the colors mean
Key Concept

Vomiting = Reverse Peristalsis

It's a protective reflex. The GI tract shifts into reverse to expel something harmful.
Red Flag

Projectile vomiting = Neuro problem

Projectile vomiting is NOT a GI origin — think increased intracranial pressure (ICP). This is a neuro red flag, not a stomach issue.
Vomiting

Prolonged Vomiting → Metabolic Alkalosis

  • You're losing HCl (acid) + potassium (K+) with every episode
  • Less acid in the body = alkalosis — the blood becomes too basic
  • Low K+ (hypokalemia) can cause cardiac dysrhythmias
Aspiration concern: Unresponsive patients who vomit cannot protect their airway. Position on side (recovery position) to prevent aspiration pneumonia.
Vomiting

What the Color Tells You

Bright Red
Active bleed, EMERGENCY. Fresh blood from upper GI.
Coffee Grounds
Slow or old bleed. Blood has been digested by stomach acid = hematemesis.
Yellow / Green
Bile. Means deep vomiting — contents pulled from duodenum.
Dark Brown / Fecal
Think bowel obstruction. Contents backing up from below.
Undigested Food
Gastroparesis — stomach not emptying. Common in diabetic patients.
Quick Check
A patient has been vomiting for 3 days. Which acid-base imbalance do you expect?
A. Metabolic acidosis
No. Acidosis would mean too much acid. But the patient is LOSING acid (HCl) with each vomit.
B. Metabolic alkalosis
Correct! Prolonged vomiting depletes HCl and K+. Losing acid shifts the body toward alkalosis (too basic).
C. Respiratory acidosis
No. Respiratory acidosis is caused by CO2 retention (e.g., COPD), not GI losses.
D. Respiratory alkalosis
No. Respiratory alkalosis comes from hyperventilation (blowing off too much CO2), not vomiting.
Section C

GERD

When the gate fails and acid goes UP
Core Concept

The Gate (LES) fails. Acid goes UP.

The esophagus was never designed for acid. When the LES relaxes inappropriately, gastric acid refluxes upward and burns the esophageal lining.
GERD

Classic Symptoms

  • Heartburn (substernal burning) — worse lying down, after meals
  • Regurgitation — acid taste in the back of the throat
  • Adult-onset asthma / chronic cough — silent nighttime aspiration of acid into airways
  • Dysphagia — difficulty swallowing from esophageal irritation
Can mimic angina! Substernal burning can look like a heart attack. Always rule out cardiac causes first.
GERD

Risk Factors

  • Obesity — increases abdominal pressure
  • Pregnancy — same mechanism
  • Hiatal hernia — stomach pushes up through diaphragm
  • Diet triggers: chocolate, caffeine, carbonation, spicy/fatty foods, citrus, alcohol
  • Substances: nicotine, steroids
  • Meds: BP meds, anticholinergics, NG tubes
Clinical Pearl

3–4 hours mouth to pylorus

That's how long food takes to clear the stomach. Don't lie down before then — gravity is your friend against reflux.
Quick Check
A patient with GERD develops a chronic cough at night. What is the likely mechanism?
A. Allergic response to stomach acid
No. This isn't an allergy — it's a mechanical problem. Acid is literally getting into the airways.
B. Silent nighttime aspiration of acid into the airways
Correct! When lying flat, acid refluxes up and can silently aspirate into the lungs/airways, causing chronic cough or adult-onset asthma.
C. Vagus nerve stimulation from esophageal distension
The vagus nerve can play a role, but the primary mechanism tested at this level is micro-aspiration of acid into the airways at night.
D. Anxiety-related hyperventilation from discomfort
No. The cough pattern (nighttime, chronic) points to aspiration, not anxiety.
GERD

Treatment

  • PPIs — suppress acid production. Take before meals.
  • H2 blockers — reduce acid (less potent than PPIs)
  • Antacids (TUMS) — neutralize existing acid (quick relief)
  • Elevate HOB — let gravity help
  • Small, frequent meals — no eating 3–4 hrs before bed
  • Avoid triggers — weight loss if applicable
Watch for: Long-term acid suppression (PPIs) can impair B12 absorption. Monitor for deficiency symptoms.
GERD

Complications

  • Esophageal narrowing (stricture) — chronic inflammation leads to scarring → dysphagia
  • Barrett's esophagus — cell changes from chronic acid exposure (next slide)
  • Esophageal cancer — serious, difficult to treat, poor prognosis
Key Concept

Barrett's = Adaptation, NOT healing

Chronic GERD causes metaplasia: the esophagus swaps its normal squamous cells for columnar cells (like the stomach). It's trying to survive the acid — but this change is precancerous.

Quick Check
A biopsy from a chronic GERD patient shows columnar cells in the esophagus. This is:
A. Normal healing from acid damage
No! This is NOT healing. The esophagus normally has squamous cells. Columnar cells are an adaptation (metaplasia), and it's precancerous.
B. Barrett's esophagus — precancerous metaplasia
Correct! Squamous → columnar metaplasia is Barrett's esophagus. It's the esophagus adapting to chronic acid, and it increases the risk of esophageal adenocarcinoma.
C. Esophageal cancer already present
Not yet. Columnar metaplasia is PREcancerous. It means increased risk, but cancer isn't diagnosed until dysplasia progresses further.
D. An incidental finding with no clinical significance
Wrong — this is very significant. Barrett's is precancerous and requires ongoing surveillance endoscopy.
Section D

Peptic Ulcer Disease

When the shield fails and acid goes DOWN
Core Concept

The Shield (mucosa) fails. Acid goes DOWN.

The mucosal lining protects the stomach wall from its own acid. When that shield breaks down, acid erodes downward into the tissue — creating an ulcer.
Connection

Same acid. Different direction.

GERD = gate fails, acid goes UP into esophagus. PUD = shield fails, acid goes DOWN into stomach wall. Know which defense broke.
PUD

Top 3 Causes

  • H. pylori (#1 cause) — communicable bacterium (oral-oral / fecal-oral). Produces urease → ammonia → destroys the mucosal shield.
  • NSAIDs (#2 cause) — block COX-1 enzyme → no prostaglandins → mucosal shield thins. Think chronic ibuprofen/naproxen use.
  • Stress ulcers — critically ill patients, can develop within 24 hrs. Cushing's (brain injury) and Curling's (burn injury) ulcers.
PUD

Additional Risk Factors

  • Male sex — higher incidence
  • Older age
  • Alcohol — irritates mucosa directly
  • Smoking — impairs mucosal blood flow
  • Steroids — weaken mucosal defense
PUD

Treatment

  • H. pylori → Triple therapy: PPI + 2 antibiotics. Must complete the full course.
  • NSAID-caused → Stop the NSAID. If NSAIDs are needed, add a PPI for protection.
  • Diagnosis: EGD (endoscopy) + H. pylori testing
  • H. pylori tests: Breath test (urea) or stool antigen
Quick Check
A patient takes ibuprofen 800mg TID for 18 months. What is the mechanism of ulcer formation?
A. Ibuprofen directly burns the stomach lining with acid
No. NSAIDs don't add acid. They remove the PROTECTION against acid by blocking prostaglandin production.
B. Ibuprofen increases HCl production
No. NSAIDs don't increase acid secretion. They weaken the mucosal shield that protects against normal acid.
C. NSAIDs block COX-1, reducing prostaglandins that maintain the mucosal shield
Correct! NSAIDs inhibit COX-1 → decreased prostaglandins → thinner mucosal shield → normal stomach acid now erodes the unprotected wall.
D. Ibuprofen introduces H. pylori into the stomach
No. H. pylori is a bacterium transmitted person-to-person (oral-oral / fecal-oral). It has nothing to do with NSAIDs.

Emergency

Perforation = Surgical Emergency

When the ulcer erodes through ALL layers of the stomach wall, acid and bacteria spill into the sterile peritoneal cavity → peritonitis.
PUD Complications

Signs of Peritonitis

  • Rigid, board-like abdomen — involuntary guarding, rock-hard on palpation
  • Rebound tenderness — pain worsens when you release pressure
  • Fever — infection in the peritoneal cavity
  • Tachycardia — the body is in crisis
This is a surgical emergency. Acid + bacteria in a sterile space = rapid sepsis. Do not delay.

Quick Check
A patient with PUD suddenly develops a rigid, board-like abdomen with rebound tenderness and fever. What happened?
A. The ulcer is bleeding more heavily
Bleeding alone doesn't cause a rigid abdomen or rebound tenderness. Those are signs of peritoneal irritation, not hemorrhage.
B. The patient is having a GERD flare
No. GERD causes heartburn, not a rigid abdomen. These signs point to something far more dangerous.
C. Perforation → peritonitis (surgical emergency)
Correct! The ulcer eroded through all wall layers. Acid and bacteria leaked into the sterile peritoneal cavity, causing peritonitis. This is a surgical emergency.
D. Bowel obstruction from scar tissue
Obstruction can cause abdominal distension and vomiting, but the classic triad of rigid abdomen + rebound tenderness + fever = peritonitis from perforation.

GERD vs PUD

GERD — Gate Fails

  • Direction: Acid goes UP
  • Defense broken: LES (gate)
  • Key symptom: Heartburn (substernal)
  • Complication: Barrett's → cancer risk

PUD — Shield Fails

  • Direction: Acid goes DOWN
  • Defense broken: Mucosal lining (shield)
  • Key symptom: Epigastric pain
  • Complication: Bleeding → perforation → peritonitis
Clinical Application

Case Studies

Put it all together
Clinical Case 1
Maria, 54 years old. History of chronic GERD, obese, smoker. She's been on PPIs for years but hasn't changed her lifestyle. She returns 2 years later. An EGD with biopsy shows columnar cells in the esophagus.
What does this biopsy result mean?
A. Her GERD has resolved and the esophagus healed
No. Columnar cells are NOT normal esophageal cells. The esophagus normally has squamous epithelium. This is metaplasia, not healing.
B. Barrett's esophagus — precancerous change requiring surveillance
Correct! Chronic acid exposure caused metaplasia (squamous → columnar). This is Barrett's esophagus — an adaptation, NOT healing. It's precancerous and requires ongoing surveillance endoscopy to watch for progression to esophageal adenocarcinoma.
C. Esophageal cancer — needs immediate surgery
Not yet. Columnar metaplasia is PREcancerous (Barrett's). Cancer would require further dysplastic changes. But Barrett's does increase the risk significantly.
D. Normal age-related change in the esophagus
No. This is not a normal finding. Squamous-to-columnar metaplasia is a pathologic response to chronic acid exposure and is considered precancerous.
Clinical Case 2
James, 62 years old. Has been taking ibuprofen 800mg TID for 18 months for chronic knee pain. He presents to the ED with coffee-ground emesis.
What's happening right now?
A. Acute GERD exacerbation
No. Coffee-ground emesis means blood that's been digested by stomach acid. This isn't reflux — it's a bleeding ulcer. The chronic NSAID use is the key clue.
B. Peptic ulcer bleeding (NSAID-induced) with hematemesis
Correct! 18 months of high-dose ibuprofen blocked COX-1 → depleted prostaglandins → thinned the mucosal shield. Acid eroded the stomach wall creating an ulcer that's now bleeding. Coffee grounds = old/digested blood (hematemesis).
C. Food poisoning
Food poisoning doesn't typically produce coffee-ground emesis. That color means digested blood, pointing to an upper GI bleed.
D. Barrett's esophagus
Barrett's is a cell change from chronic GERD, not from NSAID use. Also, Barrett's itself doesn't cause coffee-ground emesis.
Clinical Case 2 (continued)
Four hours later, James develops a sudden rigid, board-like abdomen with rebound tenderness and a fever of 102.4°F. His heart rate is 118 bpm.
What complication has occurred? What is the priority?
A. The ulcer is bleeding more — increase IV fluids
More bleeding wouldn't cause a rigid abdomen + rebound tenderness + fever. Those signs mean something has spilled into the peritoneal cavity.
B. Bowel obstruction — insert NG tube
Obstruction causes distension and vomiting, but the combination of rigidity + rebound + fever + tachycardia is classic for peritonitis from perforation.
C. Perforation → peritonitis — surgical emergency
Correct! The bleeding ulcer eroded through ALL layers of the stomach wall (perforation). Acid and bacteria have leaked into the sterile peritoneal cavity, causing peritonitis. Rigid abdomen + rebound tenderness + fever + tachycardia = classic presentation. This is a SURGICAL EMERGENCY.
D. Pancreatitis — check lipase
While pancreatitis can cause abdominal pain and fever, the timeline (bleeding ulcer → sudden rigid abdomen) and rebound tenderness point clearly to perforation and peritonitis.
Synthesis Check
Patient A has substernal burning worse when lying down. Patient B has epigastric pain that improves with eating. Match them.
A. Patient A = GERD, Patient B = PUD
Correct! Patient A: substernal burning worse lying down = acid refluxing UP through a failed gate (LES) = GERD. Patient B: epigastric pain improved by eating = food buffers acid on an exposed ulcer wall = PUD (shield failure).
B. Patient A = PUD, Patient B = GERD
Reversed. Substernal burning worse lying down is classic GERD (gravity lets acid reflux UP). Pain improved by eating is PUD (food buffers the acid sitting on the exposed ulcer).
C. Both patients have GERD
No. "Improved with eating" doesn't fit GERD. That pattern is classic for PUD — food temporarily buffers acid on the exposed ulcer wall.
D. Both patients have PUD
No. "Substernal burning worse lying down" is the hallmark of GERD, not PUD. PUD pain is epigastric and often relieved by eating.
