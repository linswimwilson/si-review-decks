Section A

Stroke / CVA

When brain perfusion fails — symptoms depend on WHERE + HOW BAD
Core Concept

Symptoms Depend on WHERE + HOW BAD

A stroke in Broca’s area → can’t speak. A stroke in the occipital lobe → vision loss. Deficits map directly to the damaged region.
Warning Sign

TIA Is NOT a Mini-Stroke

Transient Ischemic Attack = same symptoms as stroke, but NO permanent damage. Why this matters: 50% will have a full stroke within 1 year if the cause isn’t found and treated.

Ischemic vs. Hemorrhagic

Ischemic (most common)

  • Mechanism: artery BLOCKED
  • Atherosclerosis narrows artery, then embolus or thrombus blocks it
  • Most common: embolus from AFib → LA → LV → aorta → carotid → brain
  • Must come from L heart — cannot originate in a vein
  • CT: no blood visible
  • Tx: fibrinolytics (within 3 hrs)

Hemorrhagic

  • Mechanism: artery RUPTURED
  • Aneurysm (weak arterial wall) tends to rupture from ↑BP
  • Blood accumulates → shifts brain tissue from midline
  • CT: blood visible
  • NO fibrinolytics — would worsen bleeding
  • Tx: maybe drain; rehab + lifestyle changes

Section B

Assessment & Treatment

Recognize fast. Treat faster. Time = Brain.
Assessment

FAST — Recognize a Stroke

F
FaceUneven / drooping on one side
A
ArmCan’t raise / arm drifts down
S
SpeechSlurred, can’t understand or form words
T
TimeCRITICAL — when did symptoms start?
Immediate workup: CT scan to rule out bleeding. CT clear + confirmed clot + within window → fibrinolytic candidate.
Emergency

3 Hours to Save Brain Tissue

From when the clot forms. CT clear + confirmed ischemic → fibrinolytic therapy (plasmin breaks the clot). 4 hrs: might work. 8 hrs: will not work.

Treatment by Stroke Type

Ischemic — Open the Artery

  • Fibrinolytics (plasmin) — within 3 hrs
  • Mechanical thrombectomy in select centers
  • Treat underlying: AFib → anticoagulate; atherosclerosis → statins, BP control
  • Rehab for deficits

Hemorrhagic — Stop the Bleed

  • NEVER fibrinolytics
  • Maybe drain the hematoma
  • Control BP aggressively
  • Rehab + lifestyle changes post-event
Quick Check
A patient arrives at ED with sudden facial drooping, inability to raise the left arm, and slurred speech. CT shows NO bleeding. Symptoms started 2 hours ago. Priority intervention?
A. Antihypertensives to reduce BP immediately
BP management matters but isn’t the priority here. Reperfusion is.
B. Fibrinolytic therapy — ischemic within 3-hr window
Correct! FAST confirmed stroke, CT clear = ischemic. Within 3 hrs = fibrinolytic candidate. Plasmin breaks the clot. Time = Brain.
C. Prepare for surgical drainage
No — surgical drainage is for hemorrhagic strokes. CT showed NO bleeding here.
D. Anticoagulants + follow-up MRI in 48 hrs
48 hrs is far too late. The 3-hour window is closing. Anticoagulation is for prevention, not acute reperfusion.
Section C

Prevention — Primary, Secondary, Tertiary

Three levels. Same framework works for every disease. Stroke is today’s example.
Core Concept

Primary Prevents. Secondary Screens. Tertiary Rehabs.

Three levels are about when you intervene, not what you do. Primary = before anything bad happens. Secondary = after a warning event. Tertiary = after established damage.
Primary Prevention

Primary — Prevent the First Stroke

Who: general population or at-risk, no stroke or TIA yet.   Goal: reduce modifiable risk factors.
BP
BP control
  • HTN is the #1 modifiable stroke risk
  • Home BP monitoring, meds, DASH diet
🩸
Cholesterol & diabetes
  • Statins, diet, exercise
  • A1C < 7% for diabetics
🚬
Smoking cessation
  • Doubles stroke risk
  • Counsel, refer, support
♥
AFib screening
  • Irregular pulse → EKG
  • Anticoag if AFib confirmed (LA embolus)
Primary prevention is the cheapest, most effective intervention — and the hardest to get patients to commit to because they feel fine.
Secondary Prevention

Secondary — Catch It Early, Stop It From Getting Worse

Who: patients who’ve had a TIA or first stroke.   Goal: prevent progression and recurrence.
🔍
TIA workup = alarm bell
  • 50% of untreated TIAs → full stroke within 1 yr
  • CT, carotid US, echo, EKG, labs
Rx
Antiplatelet / anticoagulant
  • Aspirin, clopidogrel after ischemic
  • Warfarin/DOACs if AFib/cardioembolic
⚡
Aggressive risk-factor control
  • Tighter BP targets than primary
  • Aggressive LDL lowering (statin)
  • Carotid endarterectomy if stenosis
👪
F.A.S.T. education + adherence
  • Teach patient AND family
  • Patients stop meds when they “feel fine”
TIA is not nothing. Every unexplained neuro symptom gets investigated — the “weird spell” the patient dismissed is what you’re looking for.
Tertiary Prevention

Tertiary — Minimize Damage, Prevent the Next One

Who: post-stroke with established damage (deficits, disability).   Goal: maximize function, prevent complications.
🏋
Rehab
  • PT (gait, mobility), OT (ADLs)
  • Speech therapy (aphasia, dysphagia)
😶
Swallow & fall prevention
  • NPO until swallow eval cleared
  • Hemiparesis/ataxia/visual cuts → falls
🩹
DVT & skin
  • SCDs, early mobilization
  • Prophylactic anticoag · pressure injury prevention
👪
Med recon & caregiver teaching
  • Patients leave on 5–8 new meds
  • Family is now part of the care team
Primary risk factor control never stops. BP, cholesterol, diabetes, AFib management continue for life — because the next stroke is what we’re most worried about.
NCLEX Check
A 58-year-old patient had a TIA 3 days ago and is now in the clinic for follow-up. Which nursing intervention represents secondary prevention?
A. Teaching the DASH diet because she has no history of CVD
This is primary prevention — acting before any event.
B. Initiating clopidogrel and scheduling a carotid ultrasound
Correct. Secondary = after a warning event (TIA), before full damage. Antiplatelet + workup targets preventing progression to full stroke.
C. Arranging physical therapy for residual left-arm weakness
This is tertiary — damage has already occurred; the goal is maximizing function.
D. Educating the family on seizure precautions
Unrelated to stroke prevention framework.
TIA is the body’s warning shot — treat it like an emergency.
Section D

Clinical Application

Put it all together
Clinical Scenario
Mr. Washington, 72M, history of AFib (stopped warfarin on his own — “didn’t like side effects”), HTN, ↑ cholesterol. Wife called 911: found him unable to speak clearly, right arm limp, face drooping on the right. Onset 90 minutes ago. Vitals: BP 178/96 (stage 2 HTN), HR 88 irregular, RR 18, SpO₂ 96%. CT scan: no blood visible. Neuro: alert but confused, right-sided weakness (face + arm + leg), dysarthria.
What is happening, and what is the priority intervention?
A. Hemorrhagic stroke — prepare for surgical drainage
No. CT shows NO blood = not hemorrhagic. The AFib + no anticoagulation history strongly points to embolic ischemic stroke.
B. Ischemic stroke from AFib embolus — fibrinolytic candidate (within 3-hr window)
Correct! AFib + off anticoagulation = clot forms in LA → travels LA → LV → aorta → carotid → lodges in cranium. CT clear + 90 min onset = within window. Right-sided deficits = LEFT hemisphere affected (contralateral). Priority: fibrinolytic therapy.
C. TIA — reassure and discharge after observation
No. Persistent deficits (not transient) + CT findings and acute presentation mean this is a stroke, not TIA. TIA = symptoms resolve without damage.
D. Cushing’s triad from ↑ ICP — manage pressure
No. Cushing’s triad = ↑ BP + ↓ HR + Cheyne-Stokes. His HR is 88 (not bradycardic) and no Cheyne-Stokes pattern. This is a focal stroke presentation.
Big Picture

Stroke — The Big Picture

• Symptoms depend on WHERE + HOW BAD. Deficits map to the damaged region.
• Ischemic (common) = artery blocked. Hemorrhagic = artery ruptured. CT tells you which.
• Most common ischemic source = AFib → embolus from LA. Must come from L heart.
• NEVER fibrinolytics for hemorrhagic — would worsen the bleed.
• 3-hour window for ischemic fibrinolytic therapy. Time = Brain.
• TIA is not a mini-stroke — it’s a warning. 50% have full stroke within a year if cause isn’t found.
• FAST: Face, Arm, Speech, Time.
