Section A

The Pancreas

Your body's blood sugar control center
Pancreas

Two Jobs, One Organ

  • Exocrine: digestive enzymes (amylase, lipase, HCO₃−) → duodenum
  • Endocrine: Islets of Langerhans regulate blood glucose
  • Lies behind the stomach in the retroperitoneum
  • The endocrine side is our focus here
Pancreas

Islets of Langerhans — The Key Players

α
Alpha CellsGlucagon — when blood sugar FALLS
β
Beta CellsInsulin — when blood sugar RISES (majority)
δ
Delta CellsSomatostatin — regulates insulin & glucagon
ε
Epsilon CellsGhrelin — stimulates hunger
Alpha = raises glucose. Beta = lowers glucose. Know the pair.
Core Concept

Insulin = the KEY That Unlocks the Door

Without insulin, glucose stays locked in the bloodstream. Cells starve while blood sugar soars.
Insulin

What Insulin Does

▲
Anabolic HormoneBuilds up — promotes protein synthesis & lipid storage
🔑
Opens the DoorRequired for glucose to enter liver, muscle, & adipose cells
⇄
Transports ElectrolytesMoves K+, phosphate, and magnesium INTO cells
✗
NOT Needed ForBrain, RBCs, kidneys, lens of the eye — glucose enters freely

The Glucose-Insulin Feedback Loop

Two opposing arms, one goal: keep glucose in the 90–120 range. High glucose → insulin pulls it back down. Low glucose → glucagon pushes it back up. Diabetes breaks the insulin arm.
Quick Check
Which pancreatic cell type secretes insulin when blood glucose rises?
A. Alpha cells
No. Alpha cells secrete glucagon when glucose FALLS — the opposite job.
B. Beta cells
Correct! Beta cells are the majority of islet cells and secrete insulin when glucose rises.
C. Delta cells
No. Delta cells secrete somatostatin, which regulates the balance between insulin and glucagon.
D. Epsilon cells
No. Epsilon cells secrete ghrelin, which stimulates hunger — not glucose regulation.
Section B

Diabetes Mellitus

When insulin fails, glucose has nowhere to go
Core Concept

Diabetes = Hyperglycemia from Defective Insulin

Impaired insulin production, action, or both → abnormal carbohydrate, protein, and fat metabolism. The glucose transportation system is broken.
Classic Signs

The 3 Polys High Yield

  • Polydipsia — excessive thirst (hyperosmolar blood triggers thirst center)
  • Polyuria — excessive urination (glucose in urine pulls water via osmotic diuresis)
  • Polyphagia — excessive hunger (cells are starving despite high blood glucose)
The paradox: blood is swimming in glucose, but cells can't access it. They send "feed me" signals → you eat more → blood sugar goes higher.
Diagnostic Values

Diagnostic Numbers Know These

90 – 120Normal
> 126 fasting / > 200 randomDiabetes
  • Normal HbA1C < 6.5%
  • HbA1C ≥ 6.5% = diabetes (reflects 3–4 month average)
HbA1C = glucose attached to red blood cells. Shows the big picture over 3–4 months. A single fingerstick only shows that moment in time.
Section C

Type 1 Diabetes

The immune system destroys the beta cells
Core Concept

Type 1 = Absolute Lack of Insulin

Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells. Usually childhood onset, abrupt. Cannot be prevented.
Type 1

Key Features

⚠
WHYAutoimmune — body attacks its own beta cells. Trigger: viral or environmental.
👤
WHOChildren & young adults. Typically underweight. Can occur at any age.
⚡
HOWAbrupt onset — often diagnosed after infection. 3 Polys appear suddenly.
💉
TREATMENTMUST have exogenous insulin to survive. Cannot take it orally — destroyed by digestion.

Type 1

Treatment — The Triad

  • Insulin injection therapy (cannot take orally — protein destroyed by digestion)
  • Diet: complex carbs, protein, unsaturated fats; coordinate food with insulin timing
  • Exercise: increases glucose uptake by muscles → lowers blood glucose
  • Goal: glucose 90–120 mg/dL • HbA1C < 6.5%
Food intake must be coordinated with exercise AND insulin. Mismatch = hypo or hyperglycemia.
Quick Check
A 12-year-old is newly diagnosed with diabetes after a viral illness. Blood glucose is 340 mg/dL (random >200 = diabetes). Which type is most likely?
A. Type 2 — insulin resistance from obesity
Unlikely. Acute onset in a child after a viral illness is NOT the typical Type 2 pattern.
B. Type 1 — autoimmune beta cell destruction
Correct! Abrupt onset in a child after a viral illness = classic Type 1. Autoimmune trigger destroys beta cells.
C. Gestational diabetes
No. Gestational diabetes occurs during pregnancy. This is a 12-year-old child.
D. Prediabetes
No. A glucose of 340 far exceeds the diabetes cutoff (random >200). This is full-blown diabetes.
Section D

Type 2 Diabetes

The cells stop listening to insulin
Core Concept

Type 2 = Insulin Resistance

The key doesn't fit the lock anymore. Cells resist insulin's signal. The pancreas overworks to compensate, then gradually fails. 90–95% of all diabetes cases.
Type 2

Key Features

🔒
THE PROBLEMInsulin resistance — cells won't respond. Pancreas overworks → eventually burns out.
⚖
THE LINK~90% obese. Obesity → free fatty acids → disrupt insulin receptors on liver, muscle, adipose.
👤
WHOUsually after 40. Risk: obesity, age, family hx, gestational DM hx, inactivity, certain ethnicities.
👀
THE TRAPGradual onset, often asymptomatic — may already have complications at diagnosis.

Type 2 Vicious Cycle

Why it’s a cycle, not a line: high insulin downregulates its own receptors → more resistance → pancreas works harder → more insulin. Glucose can stay normal for YEARS during the compensation phase. Eventually the beta cells burn out and the system fails.
Type 2

Treatment — Lifestyle First

  • Weight control and diet
  • Exercise (↑ glucose uptake AND insulin sensitivity)
  • Oral hypoglycemics (↑ insulin production or cell sensitivity)
  • Insulin may be needed as disease progresses

Type 1 vs. Type 2

Type 1 Diabetes

  • Age: Usually children / young adults
  • Onset: Abrupt
  • Cause: Autoimmune beta cell destruction
  • Insulin: Low or absent
  • Treatment: Insulin (required)
  • Body type: Often underweight
  • Emergency: DKA

Type 2 Diabetes

  • Age: Usually after 40
  • Onset: Gradual, insidious
  • Cause: Insulin resistance
  • Insulin: Normal or high initially
  • Treatment: Diet, exercise, oral meds → insulin later
  • Body type: Often obese
  • Emergency: HHNK
Quick Check
Which statement BEST distinguishes Type 2 from Type 1?
A. Type 2 always requires insulin injections
No. Type 2 often starts with oral medications and lifestyle changes. Insulin may be needed later as beta cells burn out.
B. Type 2 involves autoimmune destruction of beta cells
No. Autoimmune destruction is TYPE 1. Type 2 is about resistance, not destruction.
C. Type 2 begins with insulin resistance, often linked to obesity
Correct! Type 2 = cells resist insulin's signal. Strongly linked to obesity. ~90-95% of all diabetes.
D. Type 2 only occurs in children
Opposite. Type 2 is typically adult-onset (though it's increasingly seen in obese adolescents).
Section E

Acute Complications

The emergencies that can kill
Red Flag

Hypoglycemia Is the Most IMMEDIATE Danger

The brain needs a constant supply of glucose. No glucose = seizures, coma, death.
Emergency

Hypoglycemia — Too Little Sugar Emergency

  • Causes: not eating enough, vomiting, diarrhea (not absorbed), excessive exercise, too much diabetic medicine, infection
  • Signs (HANGRY): hungry, cold, grumpy, confused — plus shaky & sweaty
  • If untreated → seizure, coma, death
  • Brain does NOT require insulin but DOES require glucose
  • If awake: give glucose orally — cake frosting, cotton candy, juice (rapid pure sugar)
  • If unconscious: injectable glucagon — can’t safely swallow
Always treat hypoglycemia FIRST — it kills faster than hyperglycemia.
Emergency

DKA = Type 1’s Emergency
HHNK = Type 2’s Emergency

Same disease family, different crises. Know which belongs to which — and the key treatment difference.
DKA

Diabetic Ketoacidosis Emergency

DKA vs. HHNK — Treatment Priorities

DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis)

Usually Type 1
  • Ketones: Present (acidosis)
  • Glucose: High (300–800)
  • Key problem: No insulin
  • Breathing: Kussmaul + fruity breath
TREATMENT PRIORITY: INSULIN

HHNK (Hyperosmolar Nonketotic)

Usually Type 2
  • Ketones: Absent
  • Glucose: Very high (>600)
  • Key problem: Severe dehydration
  • Breathing: Normal
TREATMENT PRIORITY: FLUIDS
Quick Check
Type 1 patient: glucose 450 (normal: 90–120), fruity breath, rapid deep breathing. What is happening?
A. HHNK
No. HHNK has no ketones/acidosis and is typically Type 2. Fruity breath = ketones.
B. Hypoglycemia
No. Glucose is 450 — the opposite of low.
C. DKA — body burning fat, producing ketones
Correct! Fruity breath = acetone from ketones. Rapid deep breathing = Kussmaul (compensating for metabolic acidosis). Classic DKA in Type 1.
D. Normal stress response
No. This is a dangerous, life-threatening emergency requiring immediate treatment.
Section F

Chronic Complications

What uncontrolled glucose does over time
Red Flag

Glucose Above 120 mg/dL Damages Everything It Touches

Thickens basement membranes, promotes coagulation, accelerates atherosclerosis, diminishes perfusion, and impairs immune function.
Chronic Complications

Target Organ Damage

CHRONIC HYPERGLYCEMIA
Retinopathy (eyes)#1 cause of adult blindness
Nephropathy (kidneys)#1 cause of kidney failure / dialysis
CAD (heart)Accelerated atherosclerosis → MI
CVA / Stroke (brain)Vessel damage in brain
Peripheral NeuropathyNumbness in feet — can’t feel injuries
Amputation Risk (feet)Neuropathy + poor healing + poor circulation
Neuropathy means the patient can’t FEEL injuries. A small cut on the foot can become a limb-threatening infection.
Section G

Clinical Application

Put it all together
Clinical Scenario
Marcus, 8 years old, is brought to the ED by his mother. He's been drinking water constantly, urinating frequently, and has lost 5 pounds in two weeks. Blood glucose is 380 mg/dL (random >200 = diabetes; normal: 90–120).
What type of diabetes, and what are the priority concerns?
A. Type 2 — unlikely in an 8-year-old with acute onset and weight loss
The answer text is a hint! Type 2 is gradual, typically adult-onset, and associated with obesity. This doesn't fit.
B. Type 1 — childhood onset, abrupt 3 polys, very high glucose
Correct! Classic Type 1: childhood, abrupt 3 polys (polydipsia, polyuria, weight loss), very high glucose. Priority: check for DKA (ketones, ABGs), start insulin, monitor potassium.
C. Prediabetes
No. Glucose of 380 far exceeds prediabetes range. This is full-blown diabetes with acute presentation.
D. Normal childhood growth
No. Weight LOSS with excessive thirst and urination is pathologic, not normal growth.
Big Picture

Diabetes — The Big Picture

• Insulin is the key that lets glucose into cells. No insulin or resistant cells = hyperglycemia.
• Type 1 = autoimmune, no insulin, childhood, DKA risk. Type 2 = resistance, obesity, gradual, HHNK risk.
• DKA treatment = INSULIN first. HHNK treatment = FLUIDS first.
• HbA1C ≥ 6.5% = diabetes. Reflects 3-month control.
• Chronic hyperglycemia destroys vessels and nerves: eyes, kidneys, heart, feet.
