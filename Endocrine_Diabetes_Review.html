The Pancreas

Your body's blood sugar control center

The Pancreas — Two Jobs, One Organ

  • Exocrine: digestive enzymes (amylase, lipase, HCO₃−) → duodenum
  • Endocrine: Islets of Langerhans regulate blood glucose
  • Lies behind the stomach in the retroperitoneum
  • Both exocrine and endocrine — but the endocrine side is our focus here

Islets of Langerhans — The Key Players

  • Alpha cells → glucagon (when blood sugar FALLS)
  • Beta cells → insulin (when blood sugar RISES) — these are the majority
  • Delta cells → somatostatin (regulates insulin & glucagon)
  • Epsilon cells → ghrelin (stimulates hunger)
Alpha = raises glucose. Beta = lowers glucose. Know the pair.

Insulin = the KEY That Unlocks the Door

Without insulin, glucose stays locked in the bloodstream. Cells starve while blood sugar soars.

What Insulin Does

  • Anabolic (building up) hormone
  • Required for glucose to enter liver, muscle, and adipose cells
  • Lowers blood glucose by enabling glucose to enter cells
  • Also transports K+, phosphate, and magnesium into cells
  • Promotes protein synthesis and lipid storage
Insulin is NOT required for glucose uptake in the brain, RBCs, kidneys, or lens of the eye.

Quick Check

Pancreatic Cell Types

Which pancreatic cell type secretes insulin when blood glucose rises?
A. Alpha cells
B. Beta cells
C. Delta cells
D. Epsilon cells
B. Beta cells. Beta cells are the majority of islet cells and secrete insulin when glucose rises. Alpha cells secrete glucagon when glucose falls. Delta cells secrete somatostatin, and epsilon cells secrete ghrelin (stimulates hunger).

Glucose = Sugar = Energy

Derived from carbohydrates, stored as glycogen in the liver. Simple sugars spike fast and crash. Complex carbs give slow, steady fuel.

Diabetes Mellitus

When insulin fails, glucose has nowhere to go

Diabetes = Hyperglycemia from Defective Insulin Production, Action, or Both

Impaired insulin → abnormal carbohydrate, protein, and fat metabolism. The glucose transportation system is broken.

The 3 Polys — Classic DM Signs High Yield

  • Polydipsia — excessive thirst (hyperosmolar blood triggers thirst center)
  • Polyuria — excessive urination (glucose in urine pulls water via osmotic diuresis)
  • Polyphagia — excessive hunger (cells are starving despite high blood glucose)
The paradox: blood is swimming in glucose, but cells can't access it. They send "feed me" signals → you eat more → blood sugar goes higher.

Diagnostic Numbers to Know Know These

  • HbA1C ≥ 6.5% = diabetes (reflects 3-month average — better than a single glucose reading)
  • Fasting glucose > 126 mg/dL
  • Random glucose > 200 mg/dL
  • Prediabetes: HbA1C 5.7–6.4%, fasting 100–125 mg/dL
  • Normal glucose goal: 70–130 mg/dL
HbA1C = glucose attached to red blood cells. Shows the big picture. A single fingerstick only shows that moment in time.

Type 1 Diabetes

The immune system destroys the beta cells

Type 1 = Absolute Lack of Insulin

Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells. Usually childhood onset, abrupt. Cannot be prevented.

Type 1 DM — Key Features

  • Autoimmune: body attacks its own beta cells
  • Probable trigger: viral or environmental in genetically susceptible individuals
  • Usually strikes children and young adults (can occur at any age)
  • Abrupt onset — often diagnosed after infection
  • Patient is typically underweight (glucose lost in urine)
  • Absolute insulin deficiency → MUST have exogenous insulin to survive

Type 1 Treatment — The Triad

  • Insulin injection therapy (cannot take insulin orally — destroyed by digestion)
  • Diet: complex carbs, protein, unsaturated fats; coordinate food with insulin timing
  • Exercise: increases glucose uptake by muscles → lowers blood glucose
  • Self-monitoring blood glucose
  • Goal: glucose 70–130 mg/dL
Food intake must be coordinated with exercise AND insulin administration. Mismatch = hypo or hyperglycemia.
Quick Check

Type 1 Presentation

A 12-year-old is newly diagnosed with diabetes after a viral illness. Their blood glucose is 340 mg/dL. Which type is most likely, and why?
A. Type 2 — insulin resistance from obesity
B. Type 1 — autoimmune beta cell destruction triggered by viral illness
C. Gestational diabetes — pregnancy-related
D. Prediabetes — blood sugar is only slightly elevated
B. Type 1 — autoimmune beta cell destruction triggered by viral illness. Abrupt onset in a child after a viral illness is a classic Type 1 presentation. Type 2 is unlikely with acute onset in a 12-year-old. Gestational diabetes requires pregnancy. A glucose of 340 far exceeds the prediabetes range (100–125 fasting).

Type 2 Diabetes

The cells stop listening to insulin

Type 2 = Insulin Resistance

The key doesn't fit the lock anymore. Cells resist insulin's signal. The pancreas overworks to compensate, then gradually fails. 90–95% of all diabetes cases.

Type 2 DM — Key Features

  • Usually begins as insulin resistance — cells won't respond to insulin normally
  • Pancreas compensates by producing MORE insulin → eventually burns out
  • ~90% of Type 2 patients are obese
  • Obesity → free fatty acids → disrupt insulin receptors on liver, muscle, adipose cells
  • Gradual onset, often asymptomatic — may already have complications at diagnosis
  • Risk factors: obesity, age, family history, gestational DM history, physical inactivity, certain ethnicities

Type 2 Treatment — Lifestyle First

  • Weight control and diet
  • Exercise (increases glucose uptake AND insulin sensitivity)
  • Oral hypoglycemic agents (increase insulin production or cell sensitivity)
  • Insulin replacement may be needed as disease progresses
  • Goal: maintain optimal blood glucose levels

Which statement best distinguishes Type 2 from Type 1 diabetes?
A. Type 2 always requires insulin injections
B. Type 2 involves autoimmune destruction of beta cells

C. Type 2 begins with insulin resistance, often linked to obesity. Type 2 often starts with oral medications, not insulin (A is wrong). Autoimmune beta cell destruction is Type 1 (B is wrong). Type 2 is typically adult-onset, not childhood (D is wrong).

Type 1 vs. Type 2

Type 1 Diabetes

  • Age: Usually children / young adults
  • Onset: Abrupt
  • Cause: Autoimmune beta cell destruction
  • Insulin levels: Low or absent
  • Treatment: Insulin (required)
  • Body type: Often underweight
  • Complication: DKA

Type 2 Diabetes

  • Age: Usually after 40
  • Onset: Gradual, insidious
  • Cause: Insulin resistance
  • Insulin levels: Normal or high initially
  • Treatment: Diet, exercise, oral meds → insulin later
  • Body type: Often obese
  • Complication: HHNK

Acute Complications

The emergencies that can kill

Hypoglycemia Is the Most IMMEDIATE Danger

The brain needs constant glucose — its energy demand is TWICE that of other cells. No glucose = seizures, coma, death.

Hypoglycemia — Too Little Sugar Emergency

  • Causes: excessive insulin dose, inadequate food intake, strenuous exercise, vomiting
  • Signs: shaky, sweaty, confused, dizzy, hungry, headache, irritable
  • Stimulates the sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight symptoms)
  • Brain does NOT require insulin but DOES require glucose
  • Treatment: give glucose (juice, glucose tabs) or glucagon injection
Can result in seizures, coma, and death if untreated. Always treat hypoglycemia FIRST — it kills faster than hyperglycemia.

DKA = Type 1's Emergency
HHNK = Type 2's Emergency

Same disease family, different crises. Know which belongs to which — and the key treatment difference.

DKA — Diabetic Ketoacidosis (Type 1) Emergency

  • No insulin → cells can't use glucose → body burns FAT for energy
  • Fat breakdown (lipolysis) → ketones (acetoacetic acid, acetone)
  • Ketones accumulate → overwhelm buffers → metabolic ACIDOSIS
  • Kussmaul respirations (rapid, deep breathing) — body tries to blow off CO₂/acetone
  • Fruity/acetone breath odor
  • High glucose + high potassium + acidosis + dehydration
Treatment priority: INSULIN first, then fluids. The problem is lack of insulin.

HHNK — Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar Nonketotic Syndrome (Type 2)

  • Glucose is VERY high (often >600 mg/dL) with severe dehydration
  • NO significant ketones (some insulin is present — enough to prevent fat breakdown)
  • Marked dehydration, poor tissue perfusion, decreased cardiac output, renal impairment
  • Often triggered by illness, infection, or stress in an elderly Type 2 patient
Treatment priority: FLUIDS first, then potassium, magnesium, insulin. The problem is dehydration.

DKA vs. HHNK

DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis)

  • Type: Usually Type 1
  • Ketones: Present (acidosis)
  • Glucose: High (300–800)
  • Key problem: No insulin
  • Breathing: Kussmaul
  • Treatment priority: INSULIN

HHNK (Hyperosmolar Nonketotic)

  • Type: Usually Type 2
  • Ketones: Absent
  • Glucose: Very high (>600)
  • Key problem: Severe dehydration
  • Breathing: Normal
  • Treatment priority: FLUIDS
Quick Check

Acute Complications

A patient with Type 1 diabetes presents with glucose of 450, fruity breath, and rapid deep breathing. What is happening?
A. Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar nonketotic syndrome
B. Hypoglycemia from too much insulin
C. Diabetic ketoacidosis — body burning fat, producing ketones
D. Normal stress response to illness
C. Diabetic ketoacidosis. Fruity breath = acetone from ketone production. Rapid deep breathing = Kussmaul respirations (compensating for metabolic acidosis). This is classic DKA in a Type 1 patient. HHNK has no ketones/acidosis and is typically Type 2. Glucose of 450 rules out hypoglycemia. These are dangerous signs requiring emergency treatment, not a normal stress response.

Chronic Complications

What uncontrolled glucose does over time

Glucose Above 120 mg/dL Damages Everything It Touches

Thickens basement membranes, promotes coagulation, accelerates atherosclerosis, diminishes perfusion, and impairs immune function.

Microvascular Complications — Small Vessel Damage

  • Retinopathy (eyes) — leading cause of blindness in adults
  • Nephropathy (kidneys) — leading cause of kidney failure requiring dialysis
  • High HbA1C prevents RBCs from releasing O₂ efficiently
  • Thickened basement membranes impair nutrient/waste exchange

Macrovascular & Neuropathic Complications

  • Coronary artery disease → heart attacks
  • Cerebrovascular disease → strokes
  • Peripheral vascular disease → poor circulation to extremities
  • Neuropathy: numbness, tingling, pain (especially feet)
  • Delayed wound healing + neuropathy + poor circulation = AMPUTATION risk
Neuropathy means the patient can't FEEL injuries. A small cut on the foot can become a limb-threatening infection.
Quick Check

Chronic Complications

A diabetic patient has numbness in both feet and a non-healing wound on the left great toe. Which complication category is this?
A. Microvascular — retinopathy
B. Macrovascular — coronary artery disease
C. Neuropathy + peripheral vascular disease
D. Diabetic ketoacidosis
C. Neuropathy + peripheral vascular disease. Numbness = neuropathy (nerve damage). Non-healing wound = poor perfusion from peripheral vascular disease. Retinopathy affects the eyes (A is wrong). Coronary artery disease affects the heart (B is wrong). DKA is an acute metabolic emergency, not a chronic complication (D is wrong).

Clinical Scenario

Meet Marcus

Marcus, 8 years old, is brought to the ED by his mother. He's been drinking water constantly, urinating frequently, and has lost 5 pounds in two weeks. His blood glucose is 380 mg/dL.

What type of diabetes is this? What are the priority concerns?
A. Type 2 — unlikely in an 8-year-old with acute onset and weight loss
B. Type 1 — childhood onset, abrupt 3 polys, very high glucose

B. Type 1 diabetes. Classic presentation: childhood onset, abrupt 3 polys (polydipsia, polyuria, weight loss), very high glucose. Priority: check for DKA (ketones, ABGs), start insulin, monitor potassium.
Clinical Scenario

Meet Patricia

Patricia, 62 years old, obese, found confused at home. Blood glucose is 820 mg/dL. No ketones in urine. Severely dehydrated with poor skin turgor.

What emergency is this? What is the treatment priority?
A. DKA — no ketones present, which rules out DKA
B. Hypoglycemia — glucose is 820, the opposite of low
C. HHNK — extremely high glucose, no ketones, severe dehydration
D. Stroke — confusion is present, but glucose and dehydration picture points to HHNK
C. HHNK (Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar Nonketotic Syndrome). Extremely high glucose, no ketones, severe dehydration in an elderly obese patient = classic HHNK. Treatment priority: FLUIDS first (massive rehydration), then electrolyte replacement, then insulin.

Chapter 10 Diabetes — The Big Picture

• Insulin is the key that lets glucose into cells. No insulin or resistant cells = hyperglycemia.
• Type 1 = autoimmune, no insulin, childhood, DKA risk. Type 2 = resistance, obesity, gradual, HHNK risk.
• DKA treatment = INSULIN first. HHNK treatment = FLUIDS first.
• HbA1C ≥ 6.5% = diabetes. Reflects 3-month control.
• Chronic hyperglycemia destroys vessels and nerves: eyes, kidneys, heart, feet.
