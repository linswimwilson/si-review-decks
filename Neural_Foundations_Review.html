Section A

Neurons & Their Fuel

The brain is precious — and fragile. Start with what neurons need to stay alive, and how they fire.
Neurons

Neurons Need Constant Fuel

O₂
Continuous O₂ — or they die
  • Neurons need a constant supply of oxygen
  • Any interruption → damage in minutes
  • This is why the stroke treatment window is hours, not days
🍬
Glucose — the brain’s fuel
  • Neurons also need a steady supply of glucose
  • Hypoglycemia → altered mental status, seizures
CNS
CNS neurons cannot regenerate
  • Once a CNS neuron dies, it is not replaced
  • Severed spinal cord → paralysis + sensation loss below injury
  • Other neurons may assume the role — but the original is gone
PNS
Side note — peripheral nerves CAN regenerate
  • Severed peripheral nerves can reestablish connections over time
  • CNS is the one that doesn’t get a second chance

Neurons fire through Na⁺ and K⁺ movement across the membrane. When this electrical rhythm is disrupted — by trauma, ischemia, low glucose, or electrolyte imbalance — you get a seizure. We’ll come back to this in the Seizures deck.
Section B

Brain Regions & Functions

You know what guards the brain. Now learn what it does — so when damage hits, you’ll know what was lost.
Cerebrum

The Lobes of the Cerebrum

Frontal — motor · decisions · personality Parietal — sensation Temporal — hearing Occipital — vision
Cerebrum

What Each Lobe Does

F
Frontal
  • Thinking, decision-making
  • Primary motor area
  • Broca’s — motor speech
  • Prefrontal — personality
P
Parietal
  • Primary somatosensory
  • Interpretation of sensation (hot/cold, vibration, soft/hard)
  • Central sulcus divides from frontal
T
Temporal
  • Primary auditory
  • Wernicke’s — understand speech, comprehension
O
Occipital
  • Primary visual area
  • Visual interpretation
Teaching hook: take out the prefrontal cortex → lose personality and emotions. On the next slide we drop below the cortex.
Going Deeper

Below the Cortex (surface → depth, anatomical order)

S1
Thalamus — the relay station
  • Sensory info up to cortex, motor commands down
S2
Hypothalamus — the homeostasis hub
  • Sends messages to the anterior pituitary → body organs
B1
Reticular formation — alertness + gatekeeper
  • Controls awakeness; filters incoming/outgoing information
B2
Medulla — where vital functions live
  • HR, vascular resistance, breathing, vomiting
  • Injury here = death faster.
C
Cerebellum — coordination & balance
  • Motor coordination, balance · impacted by alcohol
Why this matters for the next section: when ICP rises, the brain gets pushed down onto the brainstem. That’s where HR and breathing live. Lose the brainstem — lose the patient.
Quick Check
A patient had a stroke and now cannot understand what family members are saying, though their hearing is intact. Which brain region was most likely affected?
A. Broca’s area (frontal lobe)
No. Broca’s controls motor speech output. The patient could speak but wouldn’t understand — the opposite of this presentation.
B. Wernicke’s area (temporal lobe)
Correct. Wernicke’s is the speech-comprehension area. Damage → receptive aphasia: they hear sound but can’t process meaning.
C. Occipital lobe
No. Occipital processes vision. Intact hearing rules out a primary visual issue.
D. Cerebellum
No. Cerebellum handles motor coordination and balance, not language comprehension.
Section C

Intracranial Pressure

The closed box protects — until something grows inside. Then pressure has nowhere to go, and the brainstem pays the price.
Core Concept

Regardless of Etiology → ↑ICP is the Problem

Bleeding, tumor, hydrocephalus, edema, ischemia — different causes, same final pathway. If you remember one thing from this deck, remember this.
Monroe-Kellie

The Closed Box Rule

THE RULE
The skull is a closed box.
It holds three things: blood, brain tissue, and CSF. Total volume is fixed.
CLINICAL DECISION
💥
IF blood ↑ hemorrhage
→
brain or CSF ↓ · then ↑ICP
🧠
IF tissue ↑ tumor · edema
→
blood or CSF ↓ · then ↑ICP
💧
IF CSF ↑ hydrocephalus
→
blood or brain ↓ · then ↑ICP
⚠
IF nothing can compensate limit reached
→
↑ICP
Treatment logic follows the rule: Excess blood → drain it. Excess fluid → drain it. Excess tissue → remove it. One principle, three surgical strategies.
Emergency

Cushing’s Triad = ↑ICP URGENT

Three signs, together, mean the brain is failing to compensate. This is not a drill — call the physician, mobilize the team.
Cushing’s Triad — The Mechanism

Why These Three Signs, Together?

↑ ICP — the brain is being squeezed Compensation maxed out. Pressure rising inside the closed box.
↓
Brain forces ↑ BP — to push blood PAST the pressure Sign 1
Systolic rises, diastolic stays. Widening pulse pressure.
↓
Baroreceptors sense ↑ BP → slow the heart Sign 2
Reflex bradycardia. Body’s normal “BP too high, slow the heart” response.
↓
Brainstem respiratory centers compressed → breathing breaks Sign 3
Cheyne-Stokes: rapid → deep → shallow → none → repeat.
Three signs, one story — the brain trying to save itself. This specific trio doesn’t happen in sepsis, shock, or anything else, which is why it points straight at ↑ICP.
Late Signs

When Compensation Fails

✋
Decorticate posture
  • Hands pulled to center — toward the CORE
  • Cortical damage
🤛
Decerebrate posture
  • Arms extended AWAY from core
  • Brainstem damage — worse than decorticate
🌋
Projectile vomiting
  • Sudden, forceful
  • Brainstem signal
👁
Fixed & dilated pupils
  • Non-recoverable
  • Herniation under way
👶
Baby: bulging fontanelle
  • Pediatric ↑ICP sign
  • Skull hasn’t fused — pressure pushes the soft spot out
Herniation into the foramen magnum → brain forced onto brainstem → irreversible → death. These late signs mean herniation is imminent or in progress.

Decorticate vs. Decerebrate Posturing

Decorticate

  • Arms flexed, hands pulled toward chest (toward the CORE)
  • Legs extended, feet internally rotated
  • Indicates cortical damage
  • Relatively better prognosis of the two
  • Memory hook: “De-CORE-ticate — toward the core”

Decerebrate

  • Arms extended and rotated outward, wrists flexed
  • Legs extended
  • Indicates brainstem damage
  • Worse prognosis — brain is losing deeper function
  • Memory hook: “Decerebrate — cerebrum out of the picture, brainstem in trouble”
Quick Check
A patient 12 hours post-TBI is now showing BP 178/62 (up from 128/78), HR 48 (down from 82), and irregular breathing alternating between rapid and shallow. What is happening?
A. Early septic shock
No. Septic shock presents with hypotension and tachycardia, not widening pulse pressure and bradycardia.
B. Cushing’s triad from rising ICP — emergency
Correct. ↑BP with widening pulse pressure + ↓HR + Cheyne-Stokes = classic triad. The brain is failing to compensate. Call the physician now.
C. Normal post-TBI variation
No. This pattern is never normal. It’s the body’s last-ditch effort to perfuse a compressed brain.
D. Pain response to the injury
No. Pain typically raises HR, not lowers it. Bradycardia with hypertension points to intracranial pathology.
Section D

Bleeds & Blood Supply

The fastest way pressure rises: blood where it shouldn’t be. Where it leaks determines how fast it kills.
Core Concept

Arterial Bleeds Are More Serious Than Venous Bleeds

Higher pressure = faster accumulation = faster ↑ICP. Survival depends on getting to the source — and the skill of the facility that does.
The Layers

Meninges & CSF — the map for where bleeds live

Normal CSF: no blood cells, yes sugar. Next slide: each bleed sits between two of these layers — that’s how it gets its name.
Hematoma Types

Where Is the Bleed? (outside → in)

E
Epidural — between skull & dura
  • Arterial · fast (hours)
  • Classic: brief LOC → lucid interval → LOC
S
Subdural — below dura, above arachnoid
  • Venous · slow (days)
  • Common in elderly (brain atrophy)
SA
Subarachnoid — below the arachnoid
  • Aneurysm rupture (Circle of Willis)
  • Sudden — “worst headache of my life”
Blood Supply

The Circle of Willis

  • Two carotids (R + L) feed the circle from below
  • Circle gives rise to anterior, middle, and posterior cerebral arteries
  • The circle is a backup system — if one feeder is blocked, others can sometimes compensate
  • Aneurysms most commonly form at branch points in or on the circle — this is why subarachnoid bleeds happen here
The Circle of Willis is your bridge to the Stroke deck. Middle cerebral artery (MCA) strokes are the most common — and MCA territory maps roughly to the motor and Broca’s area. Hence classic stroke presentations: contralateral face + arm weakness with aphasia.
Quick Check
A 19-year-old soccer player is struck in the temple, loses consciousness briefly, wakes up feeling fine, then 90 minutes later becomes confused and loses consciousness again. Which type of bleed is most likely?
A. Epidural hematoma
Correct. Classic pattern: brief LOC → lucid interval → deterioration. Arterial source (middle meningeal artery near the temporal skull) = fast accumulation. Emergency.
B. Subdural hematoma
No. Subdurals are venous and slow — symptoms typically develop over days, not 90 minutes.
C. Subarachnoid hemorrhage
No. Subarachnoid presents with sudden “worst headache of life,” often without a preceding trauma and without the classic lucid-interval pattern.
D. Intracerebral hemorrhage
No. Intracerebral bleeds are usually spontaneous from HTN or stroke, not this impact pattern with lucid interval.
Big Picture

Neuro Foundations — The Big Picture

• Meninges + CSF protect the CNS. Dura → Arachnoid → Pia, with CSF bathing the brain.
• Neurons in the CNS can’t regenerate. They need constant O₂ and glucose. Neural firing runs on Na⁺/K⁺ movement — disrupt it and you get a seizure.
• Brain regions determine deficits. Location = presentation.
• ↑ICP is the unifying problem. Monroe-Kellie: the skull is a closed box. Compensation eventually fails.
• Cushing’s Triad (↑BP widening pulse pressure + ↓HR + Cheyne-Stokes) = emergency. Late signs (decorticate/decerebrate, fixed/dilated pupils, projectile vomiting) = herniation imminent.
• Bleeds: Epidural (arterial, fast), Subdural (venous, slow), Subarachnoid (aneurysm, sudden “worst headache”). Arterial > venous in seriousness.
• Circle of Willis is the vascular backup system and the site of most aneurysms. Bridge to the Stroke deck.
